\section{Inverter-Based Resources (IBR): GFL vs GFM}

As a result of the increasing demand in power systems for renewable energy technologies, inverter-based resources (IBRs) are becoming a relevant component of AC power systems. Typically, Distributed Energy Resources (DERs) produce electricity as Direct Current (DC); therefore, an inverter is required to transform the waveform into Alternating Current (AC). The inverter thus acts as the interface between DERs and the electric grid.

Due to the natural intermittency of renewable energies such as wind and solar, extracting the maximum available energy at any time has been a major objective in the integration of these assets. However, these assets are not able to provide inertia, unlike the large rotating masses of synchronous generators. Consequently, the reduction in mechanical inertia from synchronous machines has become a topic of concern for grid operators. This reduction results in larger frequency swings when the system experiences disturbances, such as load variations. These events can consequently trip loads or inverter-based units, thereby increasing reliability issues for the power system \cite{8586162}

The primary objective of IBRs is to supply active and reactive power to the grid; however, depending on their interaction with the grid, their behavior under disturbances, and the controller implementation, IBRs can be classified into two major groups: grid-following (GFL) and grid-forming (GFM) inverters.\cite{Rathnayake2021}

From an economic point of view, it is generally more attractive to install GFL inverters, since the main goal is to sell as much available energy as possible to the grid. GFL inverters can maximize energy production through algorithms such as Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT). Nevertheless, as the share of this type of inverter increases within the power system, voltage and frequency regulation become a challenge. To address this, grid operators have imposed requirements for inverters to support the grid by injecting reactive power and by adjusting their active power output in response to grid variations.

As an illustrative example, \cite{Mirafzal2020} discusses reactive power (Q) support. Whenever a voltage deviation occurs, the IBR should be able to detect and respond accordingly. In the case of a voltage decrease, the inverter must supply reactive power according to a predefined droop setting; conversely, for a voltage increase, the inverter should absorb (supply negative) reactive power.

As previously discussed, the main function of a Grid-Following (GFL) inverter is to inject active and reactive power into the grid. The distinction between GFL and Grid-Forming (GFM) inverters lies not only in the manner in which these inverters deliver active and reactive power (P,Q), but also in their fundamental control objectives. While GFL inverters rely on the existing grid to define voltage and frequency, GFM inverters actively regulate these parameters, establishing and maintaining the grid’s voltage and frequency reference.

The main distinction between Grid-Following (GFL) and Grid-Forming (GFM) inverters lies in their underlying control principles and the way they interact with the grid.

Overall, both GFM and GFL inverters share similar grid-support and power-generation objectives during grid-connected operation. These objectives are achieved either through active and reactive power adjustments in the case of GFL control, or through voltage and frequency regulation in the case of GFM control \cite{9751441}

\subsection{Grid Following Inverters}

A fundamental challenge in low-inertia power systems is that most existing inverter controllers are equipped with GFL control. 
This type of control is designed under the assumption that inertial sources, such as synchronous generators or grid-forming inverters, regulate the voltage and frequency of the grid. \cite{osti_1813971}. Another challenge arises because large PV plants or onshore wind farms are often located far from consumption centers; consequently, long transmission lines are required. As a result, the grid impedance “seen” from the IBR side tends to be high, and such areas are commonly referred to as weak parts of the grid. In these sections, maintaining voltage regulation through conventional methods becomes difficult.

Currently, the majority of PV inverters connected to the grid operate as grid-following (GFL) inverters. This type of inverter adjusts its power output by synchronizing with the grid through a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL). The PLL works by continuously measuring the angle and frequency of the grid voltage. \cite{8586162}. 

The synchronization of a GFL inverter is achieved through a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL). In this configuration, the inverter behaves as a current source, injecting current into the existing grid voltage. From a control perspective, a GFL inverter can be modeled as a controlled current source connected in parallel with a high impedance. The inverter measures the voltage at the Point of Common Coupling (PCC), $v_{PCC} $ , using the PLL. This signal is  then transformed via the Clarke and Park Transformation into the direct and quadrature (dq) components. The power control sets the desired dq currents required to inject the desired Active and Reactive Power. The Current Control source model is shown in Fig 2.1. $C_{p}$ denotes the outer power control loop and $\theta$ represents the phase angle. Under the GFL operation, only
current magnitude and phase angle in the output current are adjusted to achieve the desired active/reactive power outputs. The current source frequency remains identical to the grid frequency.

The Grid-Following (GFL) control strategy operates by delivering the commanded active $P_{ref}$ and reactive $Q_{ref}$ power based on the grid measurements provided by the PLL. 
The power control block calculates the reference currents, which are expressed in the dq-frame and regulated by a fast inner current-control loop that ensures the inverter output current follows its reference accurately. 
In this way, the inverter can be represented as a controlled current source connected to the grid at the PCC through the grid impedance, depending on the existing grid voltage to maintain synchronization and power delivery.  
During large disturbances, the GFL inverter detects changes through voltage and current measurements, which inherently introduce delays in the provision of active and reactive power support. 
From a small-signal stability perspective, since the GFL inverter relies on the PLL for grid voltage and angle measurements, its stability margin can be significantly reduced under sudden grid variations.\cite{Rathnayake2021}

\subsection{Grid Forming Inverters}

To address the increasing share of Grid-Following (GFL) inverters in power systems, the concept of Grid-Forming (GFM) control was introduced. This approach aims to enable one or more inverters to operate as voltage and frequency regulators, capable of forming a local power grid. Unlike GFL inverters, GFM control does not rely on a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) for synchronization. Instead, GFM inverters can self-synchronize with the grid by generating their own voltage and frequency references. \cite{10968238}

GFL inverters depend on a PLL to align with the existing grid, whereas GFMs autonomously establish their voltage and frequency setpoints. This capability enhances grid stability and support functions. GFM converters operate as voltage sources that internally generate their own voltage and frequency references. By emulating the inertia and damping characteristics of synchronous machines, GFM controllers provide improved frequency and voltage support. These features make them particularly robust in weak-grid, islanded, and high-renewable environments, where fast and adaptive control is crucial. Overall, GFMs demonstrate greater independence, stability, and flexibility under varying grid conditions, positioning them as a key enabling technology for future power systems with high renewable penetration. \cite{11041180}

The most common GFM control strategies include droop control, Virtual Oscillator Control (VOC), and Virtual Synchronous Generator (VSG) methods. These approaches allow GFMs to provide voltage, frequency, and inertia support to the grid while maintaining stable and coordinated operation under parallel inverter conditions. Additional functionalities have been developed to extend their capabilities, such as self-synchronization (especially for two-stage inverter topologies), which adds DC-link voltage regulation through droop functions. Other advanced features include seamless mode transition, enabling smooth switching between grid-connected and islanded operation; black-start capability, allowing system restoration after a total blackout; and coordinated control, which enables operation under unbalanced grid conditions. \cite{9751441}.

During normal grid-connected operation, a GFM inverter follows power reference signals provided by a system-level controller or a Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) algorithm to deliver high-quality power to the grid. Under abnormal grid conditions, however, the inverter can generate its own voltage and frequency based on internal control references, without using a PLL. This functionality enables direct voltage, frequency, and inertia support to the utility grid. \cite{9751441}

\section{GFM Control Methods}

Distributed Generators (DGs) are typically composed of a DC link and two main stages: an input-side converter and a grid-side converter. The input side varies depending on the specific technology and energy source used. The grid-side converter, on the other hand, usually consists of a DC/AC converter.

Grid-Forming technologies, through autonomous adjustment of their output voltage and frequency, ensure seamless operation regardless of dynamic load conditions or variations in solar irradiance — for instance, in photovoltaic grid-forming plants. These inverters operate based on fundamental control principles that include power regulation, frequency control, and voltage support.\cite{10755449}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Power control strategy: GFM control algorithms control the active and reactive power outputs, in this way, devices can balance effectively power generation and demand when demand or generation fluctuate.
    \item Frequency Regulation: GFM adjust autonomously grid frequency, in case of frequency deviatons, GFM modify its output to restore the grid frequency nominal value.
    \item Voltage control: By controlling reactive power flow, GFMs are able to provide grid support during transient conditions and ensure voltage stability. 
\end{itemize}

The primary objective in controller design, regardless of the selected control method, is to meet the requirements established by grid codes. These requirements ensure the continuous and stable operation of the power converter under both steady-state and transient conditions. \cite{10620991}

\subsection{Droop Control}

The droop control method emulates the speed-droop mechanism of a synchronous generator. It is derived from the concept of primary frequency control and enables power sharing proportional to each converter's nominal power. In this control strategy, an inverse relationship exists between frequency deviation and active power variation. \cite{10620991}

Droop control is one of the most mature and widely implemented Grid-Forming control methods. It adjusts the frequency and amplitude of the reference voltage through the \(P\text{–}f\) and \(Q\text{–}V\) droop loops, respectively. The main purpose is to emulate the voltage and frequency regulation behavior of synchronous generators. However, the rotor dynamics described by the swing equation—which represent the mechanical motion of large rotating machines—are not reproduced. As a result, droop-controlled inverters cannot inherently provide inertia support.

The frequency and voltage dynamics within the droop control loop can be expressed as follows: \cite{10949993}

\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\omega &= \omega_{\text{ref}} + K_P (P_{\text{ref}} - P) \\
V &= V_{\text{ref}} + K_Q (Q_{\text{ref}} - Q)
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

 \centering

 \caption{Block diagram of frequency and voltage droop control. \cite{10620991}}
 \label{fig:Block Diagram frequency and voltage droop control}
\end{figure}

In the equations above, \(\omega\) and \(V\) represent the inverter’s output frequency and voltage magnitude, respectively. \(P\) and \(Q\) correspond to the measured active and reactive power, while \(P_{\text{ref}}\) and \(Q_{\text{ref}}\) denote their reference values. The terms \(K_P\) and \(K_Q\) are the frequency and voltage droop coefficients.

Droop-based control uses voltage and frequency characteristics to regulate power sharing among multiple inverters. This feature is highly beneficial for decentralized systems, as it enables proper power distribution without the need for explicit communication or centralized synchronization, resulting in a more robust and scalable control architecture. \cite{10755449}

\subsection{Virtual Oscillator Control}

This control technique utilizes virtual oscillator models to synchronize multiple grid-forming devices without relying on external signals. This advantage enables independent synchronization, making GFM inverters compatible with a wide range of grid conditions. However, it has been observed that this method is highly sensitive to tuning parameters and offers a limited synchronization range. \cite{10755449}

In a conventional digitally controlled PWM inverter, the controller samples the output voltage and current and adjusts them through a double closed-loop structure with PI controllers to follow a predefined reference. In contrast, Virtual Oscillator Control (VOC) internally generates its own reference waveform using a virtual sinusoidal oscillator. When the inverter output follows this internally generated signal, it behaves as if it were a physical oscillator. This self-synchronizing behavior represents the fundamental principle of VOC. \cite{7512614}

 \label{fig:Virtual Resonance Generators}

In Fig.~\ref{fig:Virtual Resonance Generators}, \( u(t) \) denotes the capacitor voltage of the LC electromagnetic oscillator. The VOC is a nonlinear control technique designed to reproduce the dynamics of a nonlinear oscillator. The virtual oscillator consists of a virtual resistor, capacitor, and inductor, together with a voltage-dependent current source. Scaled quantities of the inverter current and capacitor voltage are used to implement the VOC, where the capacitor voltage serves as the control signal in the pulse width modulation (PWM) process to produce the terminal voltage. The controller is a decentralized version of the Van der Pol oscillator, composed of a parallel connection of a virtual inductor, capacitor, conductor, and a cubic voltage-dependent current source. \cite{Rathnayake2021}

Unlike droop control, which requires complex active and reactive power calculations and therefore exhibits slower transient performance, VOC-controlled inverters operate without explicit power calculations. In parallel systems, active and reactive power are not direct control variables, allowing for a faster transient response. \cite{7512614}

Furthermore, VOC behavior converges to that of droop control under steady-state conditions. In addition, due to its time-domain nature, VOC provides faster and more stable transient dynamics compared to the traditional droop control approach. \cite{9479398}

\section{Power System Stability}

Electric power systems are undergoing a transformation due to the rapid increase of power-electronic-interfaced technologies. As a result, new considerations regarding stability phenomena must be addressed. Power system stability follows the same mathematical foundations as any dynamic system. \cite{1318675}

In the past, the dynamic behavior of power systems was mainly governed by synchronous generator dynamics. However, modern grids have changed significantly with the increasing integration of inverter-based resources (IBRs), such as wind and photovoltaic (PV) generation, Flexible AC Transmission Systems (FACTS), High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) links, and power-electronic-interfaced loads, including Electric Vehicles (EVs) and Heat Pumps (HPs). \cite{9286772}

Stability in an electric power system is defined with respect to its motion around an equilibrium operating point. Power systems are continuously subjected to disturbances of various magnitudes. Small disturbances, such as load variations, occur constantly and the system must adapt without losing steady operation. In contrast, large disturbances—such as faults or the loss of major generating units—may induce structural changes in the network due to the isolation of affected elements. \cite{1318675}

Since it is neither economically nor practically feasible to ensure stability against every possible disturbance, contingency studies are carried out based on the probability of occurrence. With the increasing penetration of IBRs, additional challenges arise, including interactions between converter control loops, limited short-circuit current contribution, and a reduction in system inertia. \cite{9286772}

Power system stability can be defined as “the ability of an electric power system, for a given initial operating condition, to regain a state of operating equilibrium after being subjected to a physical disturbance, with most system variables remaining within acceptable bounds so that the system remains largely intact.” \cite{1318675}

Different types of disturbances excite different components of the system, each acting over distinct time scales. Figure X illustrates the typical ranges associated with these dynamic phenomena.

   \subsection{Categories of Stability}

   \begin{enumerate}
    \item Rotor Angle Stability: Is the ability of the interconnected synchronous machines in a power system to remain in synchronism under normal operating conditions and to regain synchrinism after being subjected to a small or large disturbance.
    \item Voltage Stability: Voltage Stability refers to the ability of a power system to mantain steady voltages close to nominal values at all buses in the system after being subjected to a disturbance. Depends on the ability of the combined generation and transmission systems to provide the power requested by loads and constrained by the maximum power transfer to a specific set of buses and linked to the voltage drop that occurs when active and/or reactive power flows through imductive reactances of the transmission network
    \item Frequency Stability:Refers to the ability of a power system to mantain steady frequency following a severe system upset resulting in a significant imbalance between generation and load. Depends strongly on the ability to mantain/restore equilibrium between system generation and load, with minimum unintentional loss of load. Instability that may result occurs in the form of sustained frequency swings leading to tripping generating units and/or loads.
    \item Resonance Stability: The resonance, in general, occurs
    when energy exchange takes place periodically in an oscillatory manner. These oscillations grow in case of insufficient dissipation of energy in the flow path and are manifested (in electrical power systems) in magnification of voltage/current/torque magnitudes. When these magnitudes exceed specified thresholds, it is said that a resonance instability has occurred.
    \item Converter-Driven Stability: Converter-Driven Stability (CDS) refers to the ability of a power system, in which converter-interfaced generation (and/or other power-electronic converters) is significant, to maintain operating equilibrium after a disturbance—accounting for the unique dynamics of converters and their controls. It encompasses the interactions between converter control loops (which may act on millisecond or faster time-scales) and the broader grid dynamics (electromechanical and electromagnetic). Instability within this class typically manifests as oscillatory or divergent behaviour over a wide frequency range (from sub-10 Hz up to hundreds of Hertz or more).
   \end{enumerate}

   \subsubsection{Converter-driven stability}

High penetration of converter-interfaced renewable generation, such as full-converter wind turbines and photovoltaic plants, has introduced new forms of dynamic interactions between power electronic converters and the AC grid. Unlike synchronous machines, converters lack inherent inertia and rely entirely on fast digital control loops, making their stability strongly dependent on controller tuning and grid strength. As renewable penetration increases, these interactions may excite oscillatory behavior across a wide frequency range—including sub-synchronous oscillations, poorly damped electromechanical modes, and high-frequency resonance—collectively known as converter-driven stability. Several real-world incidents have demonstrated that weak grids or high IBR penetration can significantly reduce system damping or even lead to instability. These phenomena highlight the need to analyze converter–grid interactions carefully to ensure stable operation in converter-dominated power systems. \cite{en14144290}

Converter-driven stability encompasses all stability phenomena that are influenced or caused by power converters and their associated control systems, occurring across multiple time scales. Slow interactions typically manifest as oscillations below approximately 10 Hz and are associated with outer control loops or system-level dynamics. In contrast, fast interactions arise from inner controller loops and the rapid response of power electronic converters, spanning frequencies from several tens of hertz up to hundreds or even thousands of hertz. The relevant time scales and associated phenomena are illustrated in Fig. X. \cite{10497578}

Analyzing fast converter-driven stability requires accurate and detailed models, which significantly increases computational effort. Electromagnetic transient (EMT) models, formulated using differential equations, are commonly employed to represent fast electromagnetic and control dynamics. However, because they require very small time steps to capture such rapid behavior, their major limitation is the extremely high computational burden. As system size increases, EMT simulations can become prohibitively expensive and, in some cases, practically unsolvable. \cite{10497578}

Overall, the literature highlights that the increasing penetration of converter-interfaced generation introduces dynamic interactions that demand advanced and scalable modeling approaches. While detailed EMT simulations provide valuable insight into fast converter behavior, they are impractical for large system studies, reinforcing the need for efficient methods capable of capturing inverter-driven phenomena at scale. Continued research and collaboration between academia, industry, and system operators remain essential to ensure that future converter-dominated power systems can operate reliably while supporting ongoing energy-transition objectives.

\section{RMS and EMT Approaches}
Analysis of transient stability in power systems involves computing their nonlinear dynamic response to large disturbances, typically transmission network faults, followed by the isolation of the faulted element through protective relaying \cite{kundur1994}.

A Phasor Domain Transient (PDT) simulation is a time-domain model that represents the behaviour of power system elements in a simplified and computationally efficient manner. Since the steady-state AC phasor representation of three-phase quantities can be mathematically related to their instantaneous representation, the instantaneous values of voltages and currents are not explicitly calculated. Instead, voltages and currents are represented by complex numbers—phasors—characterised by a magnitude and a phase angle. PDT tools have historically been the only viable approach for most power system studies, given their computational speed and suitability for real-time decision-making.

The RMS (root-mean-square) calculation provides a single effective value for a time-varying waveform whose magnitude changes over time. However, PDT is limited by the fact that the network retains a quasi–steady-state phasor representation. As a result, it can only represent the fundamental-frequency behaviour of network quantities and excludes the dynamics associated with network elements, commonly known as “network transients” \cite{badrzadeh2020power}.

“Transient stability is the ability of the power system to maintain synchronism when subjected to a severe transient disturbance” \cite{kundur1994}. It is strongly influenced by the nonlinear characteristics of the power system. Electromagnetic transient programs based on Dommel’s algorithm provide an alternative and more detailed representation of these dynamics.

The behaviour of an electrical power system involves both electromechanical and electromagnetic energy exchanges among its components. During normal operation, under constant load and topology, these exchanges are not explicitly modelled, and the system behaviour can be sufficiently represented using voltage and current phasors in the frequency domain \cite{arrillaga2003}.

Interactions involving the magnetic fields of inductances and electric fields of capacitances are referred to as electromagnetic transients, whereas transients arising from the interplay between mechanical energy stored in rotating machines and the electrical energy stored in the network are referred to as electromechanical transients. There is also an intermediate region in which both phenomena interact and may require an appropriate combined representation \cite{arrillaga2003}.

Transient waveforms typically contain one or more oscillatory components and can thus be characterised by the natural frequencies of these oscillations. From a modelling perspective, it is more appropriate to classify transients by their relevant time range, which is directly related to the phenomena under investigation.

\section{Frequency and Voltage Stability Indicators}

Power system stability analysis requires quantitative measures that capture how the system reacts to disturbances and how fast it returns to an acceptable operating point. Modern converter-dominated grids exhibit faster dynamic behavior and reduced inertia, making stability indicators essential for comparing control strategies, evaluating inverter performance, and assessing system strength. The following indicators are widely adopted in international standards (IEEE, ENTSO-E),system operator guidelines, and the scientific literature.


\subsection{Frequency Stability Indicators}


Frequency stability refers to the ability of a power system to maintain its nominal frequency following a sudden disturbance such as a generation outage or a load change. In traditional power systems, the electromechanical response of synchronous machines limits the initial frequency deviation through inertia, while primary frequency control determines the steady-state deviation \cite{kundur1994}. Modern converter-dominated systems exhibit reduced inertia and faster dynamics, making frequency indicators essential for evaluating system stability, inverter control strategies, and compliance with operational requirements.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Background}

System frequency is determined by the balance between mechanical input power and electrical output power. A sudden load increase causes electrical power to exceed mechanical input, leading to a frequency drop governed by the swing equation:
\begin{equation}
    2H \frac{df}{dt} = P_m - P_e,
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the inertia constant, $P_m$ the mechanical power, and $P_e$ the electrical power. 

As frequency declines, the governor responds according to its droop characteristic:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta P_m = -\frac{1}{R} \Delta f,
\end{equation}
with $R$ the generator droop. This drives the frequency toward a new equilibrium that generally differs from nominal. The resulting steady-state frequency deviation is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{\mathrm{ss}} = -\frac{\Delta P_L}{\frac{1}{R} + D},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta P_L$ is the load change and $D$ the load-damping coefficient. This behaviour characterizes the inertial dip, frequency nadir, and primary frequency control response.

\centering

\caption{Frequency Respone of a generating unit. \cite{kundur1994}}
\label{fig:Frequency Respone of a generating unit.}
\end{figure}

\smallskip
The figure illustrates the characteristic inertial frequency dip, the nadir, and the subsequent primary control recovery governed by governor droop. It is also  visible, that the frequency reaches a new steady state value. 


\subsubsection{Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF)}

RoCoF quantifies the initial frequency rate of change following a disturbance and is a key indicator of system robustness. Immediately after the event, it is approximated by \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RoCoF}\big|_{t=0^+} = 
    \frac{\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}}{P_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \cdot \frac{f_0}{2H},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}$ is the generation-load imbalance, $P_{\mathrm{LOAD}}$ the system load, and $f_0$ the nominal frequency.  

ENTSO-E considers maximum RoCoF withstand capability a fundamental operational requirement and notes that high RoCoF values may lead to generator tripping, loss-of-synchronism, or malfunction of protection devices \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}. RoCoF limits in Continental Europe typically range from 0.5 to 2~Hz/s depending on system conditions \cite{entsoe2016_freq}.

The minimum system inertia needed to contain RoCoF within an allowable limit is given by:
\begin{equation}
    T_{N,\min} = 
    \frac{\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}}{P_{\mathrm{Load}}}
    \cdot \frac{f_0}{\text{RoCoF}_{\max}},
\end{equation}
where $T_N$ is the equivalent system inertia constant.

\subsubsection{Frequency Nadir}

The frequency nadir is the minimum frequency reached after a disturbance and represents the balance between inertial response and primary frequency control. Kundur identifies the nadir as the point reached after the initial inertial frequency decline, before governor action fully develops \cite{kundur1994}.  

In regulatory practice, the rate of change of frequency (RoCoF) and the frequency nadir are defined and bounded by grid codes and system operator requirements. The Irish Grid Code defines the nadir as the minimum frequency occurring within the 5--15~s window following an event; if the absolute minimum lies outside this interval, the nadir is instead taken as the lowest value within that window \cite{EirGrid2025_GridCode}. Other jurisdictions rely on different operational definitions. ENTSO-E focuses on maintaining RoCoF withstand capabilities, typically between 0.5 and 1~Hz/s,Power with nadir requirements tied to the avoidance of UFLS activation rather than a fixed time window \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}. In Germany, VDE-FNN requirements specify that generating units must remain connected for RoCoF values up to approximately 2--2.5~Hz/s, with nadir limits coordinated with national UFLS thresholds \cite{VDEFNN2019_TechnicalConnection}. Great Britain mandates that generators withstand RoCoF values up to $\pm 1$~Hz/s for at least one second, while nadir constraints are linked to system security criteria rather than temporal definitions \cite{NGESO2022_GridCode}. In low-inertia systems such as South Australia, AEMO requires generators to tolerate RoCoF levels up to 4~Hz/s, with nadir performance evaluated against the Frequency Operating Standards \cite{AEMO2020_FOS}. These differing definitions illustrate the diversity of frequency stability criteria globally and highlight the operational importance of nadir and RoCoF in modern low-inertia grids.


\subsubsection{Steady-State Frequency Deviation}

Following primary frequency control, the system settles at a new equilibrium frequency that depends on generator droop and load damping. This steady-state deviation is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{\mathrm{ss}} = 
    -\frac{\Delta P_L}{\frac{1}{R} + D}.
\end{equation}
This indicator is essential for assessing compliance with allowable frequency ranges (e.g., 49.8--50.2~Hz in Continental Europe).

\subsubsection{Aggregate Frequency: Centre of Inertia (COI)}

The aggregate frequency of a multi-machine system is often represented using the Centre of Inertia (COI) model, which provides an inertia-weighted average frequency. The COI rotor angle is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{COI}} = 
    \frac{1}{H_T} \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i \delta_i,
\end{equation}
where $H_i$ and $\delta_i$ are the inertia constant and rotor angle of generator $i$, and $H_T = \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i$.  
When nominal generator powers $S_{n,i}$ are included, the COI angle becomes:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{COI},S_n} = 
    \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i \delta_i S_{n,i}}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i S_{n,i}}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding COI frequency is:
\begin{equation}
    f_{\mathrm{COI},S_n} = 
    \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i f_i S_{n,i}}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i S_{n,i}}.
\end{equation}
The COI representation greatly simplifies the study of the aggregate frequency behaviour of synchronous generators, especially under large disturbances.

Overall, RoCoF, nadir, steady-state deviation, and COI frequency are quantitative measures used to evaluate frequency stability in modern power systems and are crucial for analysing the impact of reduced inertia and high renewable penetration.


\subsection{Voltage Stability Indicators}
\subsubsection{Theoretical Background}

Voltage stability concerns the ability of a power system to maintain acceptable voltages at all buses under normal conditions and following disturbances. The underlying cause of voltage instability is the inability of the system to supply the reactive power required by the loads and network \cite{kundur1994}. Voltage stability phenomena are commonly classified into large-disturbance and small-disturbance voltage stability. Large-disturbance voltage stability refers to the system's capability to preserve acceptable voltages after major events such as faults or loss of generation, and is assessed using nonlinear time-domain simulations. This category may be further divided in both short-term and long-term time frames. Small-disturbance voltage stability, in contrast, analyses the voltage response to small perturbations around an operating point and can be evaluated through steady-state or linearised dynamic methods \cite{kundur1994}.

In conventional power systems, long transmission distances and high network impedances are major contributors to voltage instability because they limit reactive power transfer and increase voltage sensitivity \cite{8750828}. Additional factors influencing voltage stability include generator reactive power limits, load characteristics, reactive compensation devices, and the action of voltage control equipment such as under-load tap changers \cite{kundur1994}.

The fundamental mechanisms of voltage stability can be illustrated using a simple radial system composed of a voltage source, a transmission line, and a load. The transmission line impedance determines the voltage drop between sending and receiving ends and imposes an upper limit on the active power that can be transferred. As load increases, the receiving-end power initially rises and eventually reaches a maximum, beyond which no high-voltage operating equilibrium exists \cite{kundur1994}. This phenomenon defines the classical $P$--$V$ curve, where the nose point represents the maximum transferable power and the boundary between stable and unstable operation. The behaviour of loads also plays a central role: constant-power loads and induction motors draw higher current and reactive power during voltage dips, accelerating the transition toward instability. These transmission, generation, and load effects shape the characteristic $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ relationships and explain why weak grids—characterised by high impedance or low short-circuit ratio (SCR)—exhibit steep voltage sensitivities and reduced stability margins. In converter-dominated systems, grid-forming inverters assume the voltage-support role; their reactive current limits and control dynamics therefore determine the system’s short-term voltage recovery following disturbances.

Voltage collapse refers to the progressive and uncontrollable decline of bus voltages caused by the inability of the power system to meet reactive power demand \cite{kundur1994}.


Voltage stability can be interpreted through the classical $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ characteristics. The $P$--$V$ curve represents the receiving-end voltage as a function of active power transfer and exhibits a maximum power point, or nose point, beyond which no high-voltage equilibrium exists. The upper branch corresponds to stable operation, whereas the lower branch indicates voltage instability. The $Q$--$V$ curve expresses the steady-state relationship between reactive power injection and bus voltage. Its slope, often referred to as $V$--$Q$ sensitivity, provides a direct measure of the system's ability to support voltage through reactive power. Steep negative slopes indicate weak-grid conditions with reduced voltage stability margins. These characteristics form the theoretical basis for the voltage stability indicators analysed in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Voltage Magnitude Deviation}
The voltage magnitude deviation quantifies how far the bus voltage varies from ith nominal or pre-disturbance value. Is commonly studied for short term voltage stability. \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}
Many grid codes impose allowable ranges within $0.9 - 1.1 p.u.$, for instance, ENTSO-E Operational handbook and national gridcodes.
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_i(t) = V_i(t) - V_{i,0}
\end{equation}
where $V_{i,0}$ is the pre-fault voltage (typically close to $1\,\mathrm{pu}$). The maximum absolute deviation over the simulation window is

\begin{equation}
\Delta V_{i,\max} = \max_t \left| \Delta V_i(t) \right|
\end{equation}
Typical grid codes restrict steady-state deviations to a given band around nominal voltage \cite{rfg2016}.

\subsubsection{Post-fault voltage dip}

The post-fault voltage dip (or sag) is the minimum RMS voltage reached during and immediately after a disturbance. It is directly related to low-voltage ride-through (LVRT) requirements for inverter-based resources in modern grid codes and IEEE standards \cite{ieee1547,ieee2800}.

From the simulated RMS voltage trace $V(t)$ at the bus of interest, the minimum value in a given time interval $[t_{\mathrm{fault}}, t_{\mathrm{end}}]$ is
\begin{equation}
V_{\min} = \min_{t \in [t_{\mathrm{fault}},\, t_{\mathrm{end}}]} V(t).
\end{equation}
The relative depth of the dip with respect to the pre-fault voltage $V_0$ can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
\text{Dip} = \frac{V_0 - V_{\min}}{V_0} \cdot 100\% .
\end{equation}
These quantities are compared against the admissible voltage profiles prescribed in \cite{rfg2016,ieee2800}.


\subsubsection{Voltage Recovery Time}
Voltage recovery time characterises how fast the bus voltage returns close to its pre-disturbance level after fault clearing, or to a pre-defined band around this pre-disturbance point. It is commonly used in short-term voltage stability assessment \cite{kundur1994,cigre_vsa2024}. This indicator reflects reactive power support capability 

Given a tolerance band $\epsilon$ around the pre-fault voltage $V_0$ (e.g.\ $\epsilon = 0.05$\,pu), the recovery time is defined as
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{rec}} = 
\min \left\{ t > t_{\mathrm{clear}} \;\big|\; \left| V(t) - V_0 \right| \leq \epsilon \ \text{and}\ \left| V(\tau) - V_0 \right| \leq \epsilon\ \forall \tau \geq t \right\}.
\end{equation}
The indicator reported in this thesis is the elapsed time $t_{\mathrm{rec}} - t_{\mathrm{clear}}$ for each scenario.

\subsubsection{Voltage oscillation damping ratio}

The damping ratio characterizes how fast post-disturbance voltage oscillations decay.
It is critical in weak grids where low electromechanical damping can lead to sustained oscillations even without synchronous machines \cite{kundur1994,cigre_ibr_inertia851}.
After a disturbance, the bus voltage may exhibit decaying oscillations whose damping is influenced by system strength and control design \cite{kundur1994}. A convenient scalar indicator is the damping ratio of the dominant mode, estimated from successive peaks of the voltage waveform.

Let $x_1$ and $x_2$ denote two consecutive peaks (e.g.\ first and second maxima of the oscillatory component). The logarithmic decrement is
\begin{equation}
\delta = \ln\left( \frac{x_1}{x_2} \right).
\end{equation}
Assuming underdamped oscillations, the corresponding damping ratio is approximated by
\begin{equation}
\zeta = \frac{\delta}{\sqrt{4\pi^2 + \delta^2}}.
\end{equation}
This procedure is applied to the voltage at the PCC to compare the effect of different GFM settings and short-circuit ratios.


\subsubsection{Voltage overshoot}
Voltage overshoot measures how much the bus voltage exceeds its nominal or pre-disturbance value during the post-fault recovery. Excessive overshoot may indicate aggressive reactive power control or poorly tuned grid-forming parameters and must remain within admissible overvoltage limits \cite{anderson2003,ieee2800}.

The maximum RMS voltage in a selected post-fault interval $[t_{\mathrm{clear}}, t_{\mathrm{end}}]$ is
\begin{equation}
V_{\max} = \max_{t \in [t_{\mathrm{clear}},\, t_{\mathrm{end}}]} V(t).
\end{equation}
The relative overshoot with respect to $V_0$ is then
\begin{equation}
\text{OS} = \frac{V_{\max} - V_0}{V_0} \cdot 100\% .
\end{equation}
Both $V_{\max}$ and $\text{OS}$ are evaluated at the PCC for the different GFM parameter sets.

\subsubsection{V--Q Sensitivity}
The V--Q sensitivity quantifies the change of steady-state voltage at a bus with respect to small variations of reactive power injection. It is a classical indicator of proximity to voltage collapse and is closely related to the slope of the Q--V curve \cite{kundur1994,cigre_vsa2024}.

Formally, the local sensitivity at the operating point is
\begin{equation}
Slope_{VQ} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial Q}.
\end{equation}
In practice it is estimated numerically by perturbing the reactive power injection by a small $\Delta Q$ and resolving the power flow,
\begin{equation}
Slope_{VQ} \approx \frac{V(Q + \Delta Q) - V(Q)}{\Delta Q}.
\end{equation}
The voltage stability limit is reached when the slope of the Q--V curve becomes zero,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial Q} = 0,
\end{equation}
beyond which no stable operating point exists for increasing reactive demand \cite{kundur1994}.


\subsubsection{Critical Clearing Time}

The critical clearing time (CCT) is a classical transient stability indicator that represents the maximum allowable fault duration after which the system can still return to a stable post-fault operating point. If the fault is cleared later than the CCT, the system loses synchronism and voltage or angle instability occurs \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}.

Let $t_{\mathrm{fault}}$ denote the instant when the disturbance is applied, and let $t_{\mathrm{clear}}$ denote the clearing time. The critical clearing time is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CCT} = \max \left\{ t_{\mathrm{clear}} - t_{\mathrm{fault}} \; \big|\; \text{system remains stable after clearing} \right\}.
\end{equation}

For a simple generator, stability can be assessed via the post-fault evolution of the power angle $\delta(t)$ or relevant state variables. If $\delta(t)$ remains bounded and converges to a new equilibrium, the clearing time is stable; otherwise, instability is detected. Mathematically,
\begin{equation}
\text{Stable} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \lim_{t \to \infty} \delta(t) = \delta_{\mathrm{eq}},
\end{equation}
whereas divergence,
\begin{equation}
\text{Unstable} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \delta(t) \to \infty \ \text{or oscillations grow unbounded},
\end{equation}
indicates loss of synchronism \cite{kundur1994}. 


\section{Grid Strength and Short-Circuit Ratio}

Grid strength is a fundamental concept in power system stability analysis, describing the ability of a network to maintain acceptable voltage and frequency conditions under disturbances. According to IEEE, grid strength is typically quantified through the short-circuit power or the short-circuit ratio (SCR), defined as the ratio between the short-circuit power at the point of interconnection and the rated power of the connected inverter-based resource \cite{ieee2800}. CIGRE further refines this classification by distinguishing strong grids (SCR > 10), weak grids (SCR < 5), and very weak grids (SCR < 3), and introduces the concept of effective SCR (ESCR) to account for reactive compensation devices \cite{cigreTB871}. While classical references such as Kundur and Anderson & Fouad do not explicitly define SCR for converters, they provide the theoretical foundation for electrical stiffness, voltage sensitivity, and short-circuit strength as determinants of system stability \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}. Modern system operators have also adopted SCR as a stability metric: AEMO considers SCR < 3 critical for grid-following inverters, ERCOT requires minimum ESCR levels for HVDC and wind integration, and ENTSO-E links low SCR conditions with increased risk of oscillations, FRT failures, PLL instability, and poor voltage regulation \cite{aemo2018, ercotHVDC, entsoe2016}. In the context of converter-dominated systems, SCR becomes especially relevant because weak grids amplify sensitivity to control parameters such as virtual inertia, damping, and droop gains, ultimately shaping the dynamic behavior of grid-forming inverters under both RMS and EMT simulations. 
\begin{equation}
    SCR = \frac{S_{SC}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}
\end{equation}
    
\begin{equation}
S_{SC} = \sqrt{3} \, V_{\mathrm{nom}} \, I_{SC}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
Z_{\mathrm{th}} = \frac{V_{\mathrm{nom}}^{2}}{S_{SC}}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
ESCR = \frac{S_{SC} - Q_{\mathrm{comp}}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}
\end{equation}


The quations above summarize the basic relationships used to quantify grid strength. The short-circuit ratio in compares the short-circuit power of the grid to the rated power of the inverter. The short-circuit power, is derived from the nominal voltage and the fault current obtained from a short-circuit study, while $Z_{\mathrm{th}}$ expresses the equivalent Thevenin impedance seen at the point of interconnection. In systems with shunt reactive compensation, the effective SCR (ESCR) provides a corrected measure by subtracting the contribution of capacitive reactive power. These expressions form the basis for classifying the grid as strong or weak in subsequent analyses.


\section{Real World Examples}

Weak-grid conditions have already produced several documented operational challenges in large power systems worldwide. In the Australian National Electricity Market (NEM), multiple solar and wind plants experienced instability, poor voltage control, and FRT failures in regions with SCR values below 3, prompting AEMO to formalize weak-grid connection requirements and encourage the deployment of grid-forming capabilities \cite{aemo_2019_weakgrid}. ERCOT has reported similar issues, where HVDC links and large wind plants exhibited control interactions and voltage oscillations under low ESCR conditions, leading to minimum SCR guidelines for new IBR connections \cite{ercot_weakgrid}. Ireland has faced high RoCoF events and reduced inertia margins due to high IBR penetration, requiring EirGrid to impose operational limits and explore GFM-based solutions to maintain frequency stability \cite{eirgrid_rocof}. In the United Kingdom, National Grid observed that low-inertia and low-SCR areas exhibit reduced damping and poor dynamic performance, which motivated the procurement of large-scale grid-forming inverters through the Stability Pathfinder program \cite{ngeso_pathfinder}. Collectively, these cases demonstrate that low-SCR operation is not a theoretical scenario but a current constraint in real systems, reinforcing the need to study how GFM control parameters behave across different grid strength levels.

\section{Inverter-Based Resources (IBR): GFL vs GFM}

The increasing penetration of inverter-based resources (IBRs), mainly photovoltaic and battery systems, has significantly changed the dynamic behaviour of modern power networks. As these resources interface through power-electronic converters and do not provide mechanical inertia, system frequency and voltage become more sensitive to disturbances, particularly in weak-grid conditions with low short-circuit ratio (SCR). This motivates the need for advanced converter control methods capable of contributing to system stability \cite{8586162, Rathnayake2021}.

Two main control paradigms exist: grid-following (GFL) and grid-forming (GFM) inverters. Their fundamental differences define how each device interacts with the grid and determines its ability to support voltage, frequency, and overall system strength.

\subsection{Grid-Following Inverters}

Most utility-scale PV and wind inverters operate under GFL control. These inverters behave as controlled current sources and synchronise to the grid using a phase-locked loop (PLL), which provides the voltage angle reference. Active and reactive power are regulated through outer power loops and a fast current controller using this PLL reference \cite{8586162}.

While GFL inverters are effective in delivering programmed power, their performance depends on the external grid establishing voltage and frequency. Under low SCR conditions, the PLL interacts with grid impedance, resulting in reduced stability margins, delays in reactive current injection, or oscillatory behaviour. These limitations become more relevant in highly converter-dominated systems, where the absence of synchronous machine inertia amplifies frequency deviations and weakens voltage regulation \cite{Rathnayake2021}.

\section{GFM Control Methods}

Distributed generators (DGs) are typically composed of a DC link, an input-side converter, and a grid-side DC/AC converter. While the input stage depends on the specific generation technology, the grid-side converter defines the dynamic interaction between the inverter and the electrical network.

Grid-forming (GFM) inverters autonomously regulate their terminal voltage and frequency, ensuring stable operation even under varying load conditions or fluctuations in generation, such as in photovoltaic or battery systems \cite{10755449}. Their control framework is generally organised around three main objectives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power control:} Regulation of active and reactive power to maintain the balance between generation and demand.
    \item \textbf{Frequency regulation:} Adjustment of inverter output during frequency deviations to support frequency restoration.
    \item \textbf{Voltage control:} Reactive power modulation to support voltage stability during transients.
\end{itemize}

Regardless of the specific control strategy, GFM controllers must comply with grid-code requirements to guarantee stable operation during steady-state and dynamic events \cite{10620991}.

\subsection{Droop Control}

Droop control emulates the primary frequency regulation mechanism of synchronous machines. It introduces a proportional relationship between frequency deviation and active power, and between voltage deviation and reactive power \cite{10620991}. By using \(P\text{--}f\) and \(Q\text{--}V\) characteristics, multiple inverters can share load without communication, which makes the method robust and suitable for decentralised operation.

Although droop control can regulate voltage and frequency effectively, it does not reproduce the synchronous machine swing dynamics. As a consequence, conventional droop controllers do not inherently provide inertia support.

The droop dynamics used in GFM control are commonly expressed as \cite{10949993}:
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\omega &= \omega_{\text{ref}} + K_P (P_{\text{ref}} - P), \\
V &= V_{\text{ref}} + K_Q (Q_{\text{ref}} - Q).
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
 \centering
 \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/Block Diagram frequency and voltage droop control.PNG}
 \caption{Block diagram of frequency and voltage droop control \cite{10620991}.}
 \label{fig:Block Diagram frequency and voltage droop control}
\end{figure}

Here, \(K_P\) and \(K_Q\) denote the active- and reactive-power droop coefficients, while \(P\), \(Q\), \(V\), and \(\omega\) represent the measured quantities. This strategy enables decentralised and reliable load sharing, although its dynamic performance is limited by the absence of inertia emulation.

\subsection{Virtual Synchronous Machine}

The Virtual Synchronous Machine (VSM), also referred to as Virtual Synchronous Generator (VSG), aims to emulate the electromechanical behaviour of a synchronous machine. This includes reproducing the rotor dynamics described by the swing equation, which enables the inverter to provide virtual inertia, damping, and improved transient voltage and frequency stability.

A representative formulation of VSM behaviour is \cite{10949993}:
\begin{align}
\dot{\theta}^{ref} &= \omega, \\
\dot{\omega} &= \frac{1}{J \omega} (P^{ref} - P) + \frac{D_p}{J} (\omega^{*} - \omega),
\end{align}

where \(J\) and \(D_p\) denote the virtual inertia and damping, respectively. By replicating synchronous machine dynamics, VSM-based control avoids rapid variations in frequency and voltage during disturbances. However, tuning the inertia and damping parameters is more complex than in droop control, and improper tuning may affect stability \cite{10755449}.

The classical swing equation is given by \cite{Rathnayake2021}:
\begin{equation}
J \omega_0 \frac{d\omega_r}{dt} + D_p \omega_r = P_m - P_e.
\end{equation}

A simplified transfer function representation is:
\begin{equation}
K_{\text{vsg}} = \frac{\Delta \omega}{\Delta P}
= \frac{1}{(D_p + k_m)} \left( \frac{1}{\frac{J \omega_0}{D_p + k_m}s + 1} \right),
\end{equation}

where \(k_m\) is a gain selected according to the desired \(P\text{--}\omega\) characteristic.

 \centering
 \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/Swing based VSG.png}
 \caption{Swing-based virtual synchronous generator \cite{Rathnayake2021}.}
 \label{fig:Swing Based VSG}
\end{figure}

The ability to emulate synchronous machine inertia makes VSM-based control suitable for low-inertia and weak-grid systems, although the increased tuning complexity is a notable drawback.

\subsection{Virtual Oscillator Control}

Virtual Oscillator Control (VOC) synchronises multiple GFM inverters using oscillator dynamics instead of PLLs or explicit power calculations. The method uses an internal nonlinear oscillator that generates a reference waveform, allowing the inverter to naturally synchronize with other devices connected to the grid \cite{7512614}.

VOC operates directly in the time domain and therefore provides faster transient performance compared to droop control. It is particularly suitable for parallel operation of multiple inverters but is highly sensitive to parameter tuning and may exhibit a limited synchronization range \cite{10755449}.

VOC uses the capacitor voltage as the modulation signal in the PWM process, enabling the inverter to behave similarly to a nonlinear electrical oscillator. This behaviour allows fast self-synchronisation without requiring power calculations.

Under steady-state conditions, VOC converges to droop-like behaviour. In transient conditions, its time-domain nature results in a faster and more stable response \cite{9479398}.

\section{Power System Stability}

Electric power systems are undergoing significant changes due to the increasing penetration of power-electronic-interfaced technologies. These developments have introduced new stability considerations, although the mathematical foundations remain consistent with general dynamic system theory \cite{1318675}.

Historically, system dynamics were dominated by synchronous machines. Their inertia, electromechanical behaviour, and well-understood control characteristics provided predictable system responses. Modern power systems, however, incorporate large shares of inverter-based resources (IBRs)—including wind and photovoltaic plants, FACTS devices, HVDC links, and power-electronic loads such as electric vehicles and heat pumps. These elements modify the overall stability landscape by introducing fast dynamics, reduced inertia, and new types of interactions between controllers and the grid \cite{9286772}.

Power system stability is defined as the ability of the electrical network to return to an acceptable operating equilibrium after a disturbance, with system variables remaining within permissible limits and the network remaining intact \cite{1318675}. Disturbances range from continuous small load variations to severe events such as faults or generator outages. Since it is not feasible to guarantee stability for all possible scenarios, system operators rely on contingency-based analyses, prioritising the most probable or most critical events.

The increasing share of IBRs introduces additional challenges, such as reduced short-circuit current, interactions between converter control loops, and lower system inertia. These aspects can affect frequency and voltage stability, especially in weak-grid conditions where electromagnetic and control interactions become more pronounced \cite{9286772}.

Different types of disturbances activate system components with distinct response times. Figure~\ref{fig:Power System Time Scales} illustrates the typical time scales of relevant dynamic phenomena.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/Power System Time Scales.png}
    \caption{Power system time scales \cite{9286772}.}
    \label{fig:Power System Time Scales}
\end{figure}

IBRs exhibit significantly faster dynamic responses than synchronous machines. While electromechanical time-scale analysis allows model simplifications suitable for traditional stability studies, fast inverter dynamics—particularly those related to internal control loops or switching—are not fully captured using simplified phasor-domain models. This motivates the inclusion of faster time scales when assessing systems with high IBR penetration \cite{9286772}.

Modern power systems can thus be regarded as high-order, multivariable dynamic processes in which stability depends on operating conditions, network topology, and the type and magnitude of the disturbance \cite{1318675}. Figure~\ref{fig:Classification of Power System Stability} presents the standard classification of stability phenomena.

\end{figure}

Classifying stability phenomena enables the use of appropriate analytical methods and modelling approaches for each category \cite{9286772}.

\subsection{Categories of Stability}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Rotor Angle Stability:} Ability of interconnected synchronous machines to remain in synchronism during normal operation and following disturbances.
    
    \item \textbf{Voltage Stability:} Ability of the system to maintain acceptable voltage levels after a disturbance. It depends on generation and transmission capability, reactive power availability, and the limits of maximum power transfer.
    
    \item \textbf{Frequency Stability:} Ability of the system to maintain or restore nominal frequency following large imbalances between load and generation. Instability may manifest as sustained frequency swings or widespread tripping of units.
    
    \item \textbf{Resonance Stability:} Occurs when insufficient damping leads to energy exchange in oscillatory modes, resulting in magnified voltage, current, or torque oscillations.
    
    \item \textbf{Converter-Driven Stability (CDS):} Stability associated with interactions between converter control systems and the grid. These phenomena span a wide frequency range and are increasingly relevant in power systems dominated by power-electronic devices.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Converter-Driven Stability}

High penetration of converter-interfaced renewable generation has introduced new dynamic interactions not present in synchronous machine-dominated grids. Converters lack mechanical inertia and rely entirely on fast digital control loops, making system behaviour strongly dependent on controller tuning and grid strength. As IBR penetration increases, these interactions may lead to oscillatory or poorly damped behaviour across a broad frequency spectrum—including sub-synchronous oscillations, electromechanical modes, and high-frequency resonances. Real-world incidents have shown that weak-grid conditions can significantly reduce system damping or even trigger instability \cite{en14144290}.

Converter-driven stability encompasses dynamic phenomena influenced or caused by converters and their internal controls. Slow interactions typically arise below 10~Hz and are linked to outer control loops or system-level dynamics. Fast interactions originate from inner control loops and power-electronic behaviour, covering tens to hundreds of hertz or more. These frequency ranges are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:Frequency Range of Converter Driven Stability} \cite{10497578}.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/Converter Driven Stability Classification.png}
\caption{Frequency range of converter-driven stability \cite{10497578}.}
\label{fig:Frequency Range of Converter Driven Stability}
\end{figure}

The analysis of fast converter-driven interactions typically requires EMT models, which represent detailed electromagnetic and control dynamics. However, EMT simulations require very small time steps, resulting in high computational effort. For larger systems, they may become impractical or computationally prohibitive \cite{10497578}.

Overall, the literature highlights that increasing IBR penetration necessitates advanced and scalable modelling approaches to capture converter-grid interactions. While EMT simulations provide valuable insights, they are difficult to apply at large scale, motivating the development of efficient methods capable of representing converter-driven dynamics in future power systems.

\section{RMS and EMT Approaches}

Transient stability analysis evaluates the nonlinear dynamic response of a power system subjected to large disturbances, such as short circuits or the sudden loss of generation. In classical studies, the faulted element is cleared by protection systems, and the post-fault dynamics determine whether the system remains stable \cite{kundur1994}.

Phasor Domain Transient (PDT) or RMS simulations represent voltages and currents as complex phasors—magnitudes and angles—rather than instantaneous waveforms. Because three-phase steady-state quantities can be related to their phasor representation, RMS simulations avoid solving electromagnetic phenomena explicitly, resulting in high computational efficiency. For decades, this approach has been the standard for transient stability studies due to its speed and suitability for large-scale system analysis and operator decision support.

However, RMS models rely on quasi–steady-state assumptions. They capture only fundamental-frequency electromechanical behaviour and neglect fast electromagnetic dynamics (“network transients”), limiting their ability to represent interactions driven by power electronic converters \cite{badrzadeh2020power}.

Electromagnetic transient (EMT) programs provide a more detailed representation by solving instantaneous differential equations, typically using Dommel’s algorithm. EMT simulations capture both electromechanical and electromagnetic energy exchanges in network components, including the dynamics of inductances, capacitances, controllers, and switching elements \cite{arrillaga2003}. This makes EMT indispensable for analysing fast converter-driven interactions, resonance phenomena, and weak-grid behaviour—topics increasingly relevant in systems with high IBR penetration.

Electromagnetic transients arise from interactions between electric and magnetic fields and evolve on sub-millisecond time scales. Electromechanical transients, in contrast, originate from the dynamics of synchronous machine rotors and operate over hundreds of milliseconds or seconds. An intermediate region exists where both phenomena interact and may require combined modelling approaches \cite{arrillaga2003}. In practice, transient waveforms consist of oscillatory components associated with different natural frequencies, and selecting the appropriate modelling domain depends on the time-scale of interest.

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency range of coverage EMT and PDT studies} summarises the frequency ranges covered by RMS and EMT approaches. RMS studies are suitable for electromechanical stability assessment, while EMT simulations are required to capture fast converter and controller dynamics—particularly relevant for weak grids and grid-forming inverter analysis.

\section{Frequency and Voltage Stability Indicators}

Power system stability analysis requires quantitative measures that describe how the system reacts to disturbances and how fast it returns to an acceptable operating point. Modern converter-dominated grids exhibit faster dynamics and reduced inertia, which makes stability indicators essential for comparing control strategies, evaluating inverter performance, and assessing system strength. The indicators presented in this section are widely used in international standards (IEEE, ENTSO-E), system operator guidelines, and the scientific literature.

\subsection{Frequency Stability Indicators}

Frequency stability refers to the ability of a power system to maintain its nominal frequency following a sudden disturbance such as a generation outage or a load change. In traditional systems, the electromechanical response of synchronous machines limits the initial frequency deviation through inertia, while primary frequency control determines the steady-state deviation \cite{kundur1994}. In converter-dominated systems, reduced inertia and faster dynamics increase the importance of suitable frequency indicators for stability assessment, inverter control design, and compliance with operational requirements.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Background}

System frequency is determined by the balance between mechanical input power and electrical output power. A sudden load increase causes electrical power to exceed mechanical input, leading to a frequency drop governed by the swing equation:
\begin{equation}
    2H \frac{df}{dt} = P_m - P_e,
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the inertia constant, $P_m$ the mechanical power, and $P_e$ the electrical power.

As frequency declines, the governor responds according to its droop characteristic:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta P_m = -\frac{1}{R} \Delta f,
\end{equation}
with $R$ the generator droop. This drives the frequency towards a new equilibrium that generally differs from nominal. The resulting steady-state deviation is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{\mathrm{ss}} = -\frac{\Delta P_L}{\frac{1}{R} + D},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta P_L$ is the load change and $D$ the load-damping coefficient. This behaviour characterises the inertial dip, frequency nadir, and primary frequency control response.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/frequency response of generating unit.png}
\caption{Frequency response of a generating unit \cite{kundur1994}.}
\label{fig:Frequency Respone of a generating unit.}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency Respone of a generating unit.} illustrates the characteristic inertial frequency dip, the nadir, and the subsequent primary control recovery governed by governor droop, as well as the new steady-state frequency value.

\subsubsection{Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF)}

The rate of change of frequency (RoCoF) quantifies the initial speed of frequency deviation following a disturbance and is a key indicator of system robustness. Immediately after the event, it can be approximated by \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RoCoF}\big|_{t=0^+} = 
    \frac{\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}}{P_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \cdot \frac{f_0}{2H},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}$ is the generation–load imbalance, $P_{\mathrm{LOAD}}$ the system load, and $f_0$ the nominal frequency.

ENTSO-E regards RoCoF withstand capability as a fundamental operational requirement and notes that high RoCoF values may lead to generator tripping, loss of synchronism, or malfunction of protection devices \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}. Typical limits in Continental Europe range from $0.5$ to $2\,\mathrm{Hz/s}$ depending on system conditions \cite{entsoe2016_freq}.

The minimum system inertia needed to contain RoCoF within a specified limit is
\begin{equation}
    T_{N,\min} = 
    \frac{\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}}{P_{\mathrm{Load}}}
    \cdot \frac{f_0}{\text{RoCoF}_{\max}},
\end{equation}
where $T_N$ is the equivalent system inertia constant.

\subsubsection{Frequency Nadir}

The frequency nadir is the minimum frequency reached after a disturbance and represents the balance between inertial response and primary frequency control. It is typically reached after the initial inertial frequency decline and before governor action has fully developed \cite{kundur1994}.

In practice, both RoCoF and nadir are defined and constrained by grid codes and system operator requirements. For example, the Irish Grid Code defines the nadir as the minimum frequency occurring within a 5--15~s window following an event; if the absolute minimum lies outside this interval, the nadir is taken as the lowest value within that window \cite{EirGrid2025_GridCode}. ENTSO-E focuses on maintaining RoCoF withstand capabilities, typically between $0.5$ and $1\,\mathrm{Hz/s}$, while nadir requirements are indirectly linked to the avoidance of under-frequency load shedding \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}. VDE-FNN requires generating units in Germany to remain connected for RoCoF values up to approximately $2$--$2.5\,\mathrm{Hz/s}$, with nadir limits coordinated with national UFLS thresholds \cite{VDEFNN2019_TechnicalConnection}. In Great Britain, generators must withstand RoCoF levels up to $\pm 1\,\mathrm{Hz/s}$ for at least one second \cite{NGESO2022_GridCode}, whereas in low-inertia systems such as South Australia, AEMO specifies RoCoF withstand levels up to $4\,\mathrm{Hz/s}$, with nadir evaluated against frequency operating standards \cite{AEMO2020_FOS}. These different definitions illustrate the diversity of frequency stability criteria worldwide and highlight the operational relevance of nadir and RoCoF in low-inertia grids.

\subsubsection{Steady-State Frequency Deviation}

Following primary frequency control, the system settles at a new equilibrium frequency that depends on generator droop and load damping. The steady-state deviation is
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{\mathrm{ss}} = 
    -\frac{\Delta P_L}{\frac{1}{R} + D}.
\end{equation}
This indicator is used to assess compliance with allowable frequency ranges (for instance, 49.8--50.2~Hz in Continental Europe) and to evaluate the adequacy of primary control reserves.

\subsubsection{Aggregate Frequency: Centre of Inertia (COI)}

The aggregate frequency of a multi-machine system is often represented using the Centre of Inertia (COI), which provides an inertia-weighted average frequency. The COI rotor angle is defined as
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{COI}} = 
    \frac{1}{H_T} \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i \delta_i,
\end{equation}
where $H_i$ and $\delta_i$ are the inertia constant and rotor angle of generator $i$, and $H_T = \sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i$.  

When nominal generator powers $S_{n,i}$ are included, the COI angle becomes
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\mathrm{COI},S_n} = 
    \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i \delta_i S_{n,i}}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i S_{n,i}},
\end{equation}
and the corresponding COI frequency is
\begin{equation}
    f_{\mathrm{COI},S_n} = 
    \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i f_i S_{n,i}}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{N} H_i S_{n,i}}.
\end{equation}
The COI representation simplifies the analysis of aggregate frequency behaviour, especially under large disturbances, and is useful for comparing the impact of different inertia levels and control strategies.

Overall, RoCoF, nadir, steady-state deviation, and COI frequency are key quantitative measures used to evaluate frequency stability in modern power systems and are central to analysing the impact of reduced inertia and high renewable penetration.

\subsection{Voltage Stability Indicators}

\subsubsection{Theoretical Background}

Voltage stability refers to the ability of a power system to maintain acceptable voltages at all buses under normal operating conditions and following disturbances. Its underlying cause is the inability of the system to supply the reactive power demanded by loads and the network \cite{kundur1994}. Voltage stability phenomena are typically classified into large-disturbance and small-disturbance categories. Large-disturbance voltage stability concerns the system response to major events, such as faults or loss of generation, and is assessed using nonlinear time-domain simulations over short- or long-term time frames. Small-disturbance voltage stability focuses on the response to small perturbations around an operating point and can be analysed using steady-state or linearised dynamic methods \cite{kundur1994}.

In conventional grids, long transmission distances and high network impedances contribute to voltage instability by limiting reactive power transfer and increasing voltage sensitivity \cite{8750828}. Additional influencing factors include generator reactive power limits, load characteristics, reactive compensation devices, and the action of voltage control equipment such as under-load tap changers \cite{kundur1994}.

The fundamental mechanisms of voltage stability can be illustrated using a simple radial system composed of a voltage source, a transmission line, and a load. The line impedance determines the voltage drop and the maximum transferable active power. As load increases, the receiving-end power initially rises and eventually reaches a maximum, beyond which no high-voltage equilibrium exists \cite{kundur1994}. This defines the classical $P$--$V$ curve, where the nose point marks the boundary between stable and unstable operation. Load behaviour plays a critical role: constant-power loads and induction motors increase current and reactive power demand during voltage dips, accelerating the transition towards instability. These effects shape the characteristic $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ curves and explain why weak grids—characterised by high impedance or low short-circuit ratio (SCR)—exhibit steep voltage sensitivities and reduced stability margins. In converter-dominated systems, grid-forming inverters assume the voltage-support role; their reactive current limits and control dynamics therefore determine short-term voltage recovery following disturbances.

Voltage collapse refers to the progressive and uncontrollable decline of bus voltages caused by the inability of the power system to meet reactive power demand \cite{kundur1994}.

Voltage stability can be interpreted using the classical $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ characteristics. The $P$--$V$ curve represents the receiving-end voltage as a function of active power transfer and exhibits a maximum power point (nose point) beyond which no high-voltage equilibrium exists. The upper branch corresponds to stable operation, whereas the lower branch indicates voltage instability. The $Q$--$V$ curve expresses the steady-state relationship between reactive power injection and bus voltage. Its slope, often referred to as $V$--$Q$ sensitivity, provides a measure of the ability of the system to support voltage via reactive power. Steep negative slopes indicate weak-grid conditions and reduced voltage stability margins. These characteristics form the theoretical basis for the voltage indicators used in this thesis.

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Voltage Magnitude Deviation}

The voltage magnitude deviation quantifies how far a bus voltage deviates from its nominal or pre-disturbance value. It is commonly used in short-term voltage stability assessment \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}. Many grid codes specify allowable steady-state ranges, often within $0.9$--$1.1\,\mathrm{pu}$.

\begin{equation}
\Delta V_i(t) = V_i(t) - V_{i,0},
\end{equation}
where $V_{i,0}$ is the pre-fault voltage (typically close to $1\,\mathrm{pu}$). The maximum absolute deviation over the simulation window is
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_{i,\max} = \max_t \left| \Delta V_i(t) \right|.
\end{equation}
Typical grid codes restrict steady-state deviations to a prescribed band around nominal voltage \cite{rfg2016}.

\subsubsection{Post-Fault Voltage Dip}

The post-fault voltage dip (or sag) is the minimum RMS voltage reached during and immediately after a disturbance. It is directly related to low-voltage ride-through (LVRT) requirements for inverter-based resources in modern grid codes and IEEE standards \cite{ieee1547,ieee2800}.

From the simulated RMS voltage trace $V(t)$ at the bus of interest, the minimum value in a given time interval $[t_{\mathrm{fault}}, t_{\mathrm{end}}]$ is
\begin{equation}
V_{\min} = \min_{t \in [t_{\mathrm{fault}},\, t_{\mathrm{end}}]} V(t).
\end{equation}
The relative depth of the dip with respect to the pre-fault voltage $V_0$ is
\begin{equation}
\text{Dip} = \frac{V_0 - V_{\min}}{V_0} \cdot 100\% .
\end{equation}
These quantities are compared against the admissible voltage profiles prescribed in \cite{rfg2016,ieee2800}.

\subsubsection{Voltage Recovery Time}

Voltage recovery time characterises how fast the bus voltage returns close to its pre-disturbance level after fault clearing, or to a predefined band around this pre-disturbance point. It is commonly used in short-term voltage stability assessment and reflects the reactive power support capability of the system \cite{kundur1994,cigre_vsa2024}.

Given a tolerance band $\epsilon$ around the pre-fault voltage $V_0$ (for example $\epsilon = 0.05\,\mathrm{pu}$), the recovery time is defined as
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{rec}} = 
\min \left\{ t > t_{\mathrm{clear}} \;\big|\; \left| V(t) - V_0 \right| \leq \epsilon \ \text{and}\ \left| V(\tau) - V_0 \right| \leq \epsilon\ \forall \tau \geq t \right\}.
\end{equation}
The indicator reported in this thesis is the elapsed time $t_{\mathrm{rec}} - t_{\mathrm{clear}}$.

\subsubsection{Voltage Oscillation Damping Ratio}

The damping ratio characterises how fast post-disturbance voltage oscillations decay. It is particularly relevant in weak grids, where low electromechanical damping can lead to sustained oscillations even in the absence of synchronous machines \cite{kundur1994,cigre_ibr_inertia851}. After a disturbance, the bus voltage may exhibit decaying oscillations whose damping depends on system strength and control design \cite{kundur1994}. A convenient scalar indicator is the damping ratio of the dominant mode, estimated from successive peaks of the voltage waveform.

Let $x_1$ and $x_2$ denote two consecutive peaks (for example the first and second maxima of the oscillatory component). The logarithmic decrement is
\begin{equation}
\delta = \ln\left( \frac{x_1}{x_2} \right).
\end{equation}
Assuming underdamped oscillations, the corresponding damping ratio is approximated by
\begin{equation}
\zeta = \frac{\delta}{\sqrt{4\pi^2 + \delta^2}}.
\end{equation}
In this work, this procedure is applied to the voltage at the PCC to compare the effect of different GFM parameter settings and short-circuit ratios.

\subsubsection{Voltage Overshoot}

Voltage overshoot measures how much the bus voltage exceeds its nominal or pre-disturbance value during the post-fault recovery. Excessive overshoot may indicate aggressive reactive power control or poorly tuned grid-forming parameters and must remain within admissible overvoltage limits \cite{anderson2003,ieee2800}.

The maximum RMS voltage in a selected post-fault interval $[t_{\mathrm{clear}}, t_{\mathrm{end}}]$ is
\begin{equation}
V_{\max} = \max_{t \in [t_{\mathrm{clear}},\, t_{\mathrm{end}}]} V(t),
\end{equation}
and the relative overshoot with respect to $V_0$ is
\begin{equation}
\text{OS} = \frac{V_{\max} - V_0}{V_0} \cdot 100\% .
\end{equation}
Both $V_{\max}$ and $\text{OS}$ are evaluated at the PCC for the different GFM parameter sets.

\subsubsection{V--Q Sensitivity}

The $V$--$Q$ sensitivity quantifies the change of steady-state voltage at a bus with respect to small variations of reactive power injection. It is a classical indicator of proximity to voltage collapse and is closely related to the slope of the $Q$--$V$ curve \cite{kundur1994,cigre_vsa2024}.

Formally, the local sensitivity at the operating point is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Slope}_{VQ} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial Q}.
\end{equation}
In practice, it is estimated numerically by perturbing the reactive power injection by a small $\Delta Q$ and resolving the power flow:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Slope}_{VQ} \approx \frac{V(Q + \Delta Q) - V(Q)}{\Delta Q}.
\end{equation}
The voltage stability limit is reached when the slope of the $Q$--$V$ curve becomes zero,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial V}{\partial Q} = 0,
\end{equation}
beyond which no stable operating point exists for increasing reactive demand \cite{kundur1994}.

\subsubsection{Critical Clearing Time}

The critical clearing time (CCT) is a classical transient stability indicator that represents the maximum allowable fault duration after which the system can still return to a stable post-fault operating point. If the fault is cleared later than the CCT, the system loses synchronism and voltage or angle instability occurs \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}.

Let $t_{\mathrm{fault}}$ denote the instant when the disturbance is applied and $t_{\mathrm{clear}}$ the fault clearing time. The CCT is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CCT} = \max \left\{ t_{\mathrm{clear}} - t_{\mathrm{fault}} \; \big|\; \text{system remains stable after clearing} \right\}.
\end{equation}

For a simple generator, stability can be assessed via the post-fault evolution of the power angle $\delta(t)$ or other relevant state variables. If $\delta(t)$ remains bounded and converges to a new equilibrium, the clearing time is considered stable; otherwise, instability is detected. Mathematically,
\begin{equation}
\text{Stable} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \lim_{t \to \infty} \delta(t) = \delta_{\mathrm{eq}},
\end{equation}
whereas divergence,
\begin{equation}
\text{Unstable} \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \delta(t) \to \infty \ \text{or oscillations grow unbounded},
\end{equation}
indicates loss of synchronism \cite{kundur1994}.

\section{Grid Strength and Short-Circuit Ratio}

Grid strength is a fundamental concept in power system stability analysis and describes the ability of a network to maintain acceptable voltage and frequency conditions under disturbances. According to IEEE, grid strength can be quantified using the short-circuit power or the short-circuit ratio (SCR), defined as the ratio between the short-circuit power at the point of interconnection and the rated power of the connected inverter-based resource \cite{ieee2800}:
\begin{equation}
    SCR = \frac{S_{SC}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}.
\end{equation}

CIGRÉ refines this concept by classifying grids as strong ($SCR > 10$), weak ($SCR < 5$), and very weak ($SCR < 3$), and introduces the effective SCR (ESCR) to account for reactive compensation devices \cite{cigreTB871}. Classical references such as Kundur and Anderson \& Fouad do not define SCR explicitly for converters, but they provide the theoretical basis for electrical stiffness, voltage sensitivity, and short-circuit strength as key determinants of system stability \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}.

System operators increasingly use SCR as a practical stability metric. AEMO considers $SCR < 3$ critical for grid-following inverters, ERCOT specifies minimum ESCR levels for HVDC and wind integration, and ENTSO-E associates low SCR conditions with an increased risk of oscillations, FRT failures, PLL instability, and poor voltage regulation \cite{aemo2018,ercotHVDC,entsoe2016}. In converter-dominated systems, SCR is therefore particularly relevant, since weak grids amplify the sensitivity of dynamic behaviour to control parameters such as virtual inertia, damping, and droop gains, directly influencing the performance of grid-forming inverters in both RMS and EMT simulations.

The main quantities used to characterise grid strength are:
\begin{equation}
S_{SC} = \sqrt{3} \, V_{\mathrm{nom}} \, I_{SC},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
Z_{\mathrm{th}} = \frac{V_{\mathrm{nom}}^{2}}{S_{SC}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
ESCR = \frac{S_{SC} - Q_{\mathrm{comp}}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}.
\end{equation}

Here, $S_{SC}$ is the short-circuit power at the point of interconnection, obtained from the nominal voltage $V_{\mathrm{nom}}$ and the short-circuit current $I_{SC}$ of a fault study. The equivalent Thevenin impedance $Z_{\mathrm{th}}$ represents the electrical stiffness of the grid as “seen” from the connection point. In systems with shunt reactive compensation $Q_{\mathrm{comp}}$, the effective SCR (ESCR) provides a corrected measure by subtracting the contribution of capacitive reactive power. These relationships form the quantitative basis used in this thesis to classify grids as strong, weak, or very weak and to analyse the impact of GFM control parameters across different SCR levels.

\section{Research Gap and Motivation}
The objective of this thesis is to evaluate frequency and voltage stability in a converter-dominated power system and to analyse how key GFM control parameters—virtual inertia, damping, and droop—behave under different grid strength scenarios. By assessing parameter sensitivity across strong, weak, and very weak grids, this work aims to provide a clear understanding of how GFM tuning affects system-level stability indicators. A brief overview of market-related considerations is also included to contextualize the practical relevance of GFM operation. Ultimately, the goal is to provide RWE with a consistent and transparent assessment framework to quantify the impact of GFM parameters on system stability and to offer insights into how these findings may influence future grid market designs and RWE's potential participation in them.
