\section{Inverter-Based Resources (IBR): GFL vs GFM}

The increasing penetration of inverter-based resources (IBRs), mainly photovoltaic and battery systems, has significantly changed the dynamic behaviour of modern power networks. As these resources interface through power-electronic converters and do not provide mechanical inertia, system frequency and voltage become more sensitive to disturbances, particularly in weak-grid conditions with low short-circuit ratio (SCR). This motivates the need for advanced converter control methods capable of contributing to system stability \cite{8586162, Rathnayake2021}.

Two main control paradigms exist: grid-following (GFL) and grid-forming (GFM) inverters. Their fundamental differences determine their capability to support system stability.

\subsection{Grid-Following Inverters}

Most utility-scale PV and wind inverters operate under GFL control. These inverters behave as controlled current sources and synchronise to the grid using a phase-locked loop (PLL), which provides the voltage angle reference. Active and reactive power are regulated through outer power loops and a fast current controller using this PLL reference \cite{8586162}.

While GFL inverters are effective in delivering programmed power, their performance depends on the external grid establishing voltage and frequency. Under low SCR conditions, the PLL interacts with grid impedance, resulting in reduced stability margins, delays in reactive current injection, or oscillatory behaviour. These limitations become more relevant in highly converter-dominated systems, where the absence of synchronous machine inertia amplifies frequency deviations and weakens voltage regulation \cite{Rathnayake2021}.

\subsection{Grid-Forming Inverters}

GFM inverters operate fundamentally differently. Instead of relying on the grid to establish voltage and frequency, they behave as controlled voltage sources and generate their own internal voltage and frequency references without a PLL. This allows them to regulate frequency and voltage directly and provide synthetic inertia, damping, and fast fault response. Their behaviour is therefore more robust in weak-grid and low-inertia conditions \cite{9751441, 11041180}.

 \label{fig:GFM Control Structure}

Common GFM control strategies include droop control, virtual synchronous machine (VSM), and virtual oscillator control (VOC). These methods aim to provide fast frequency support, synthetic inertia, voltage regulation, improved damping, black-start capability, and stable operation under low SCR. 

The distinction between GFL and GFM is fundamental for stability assessment in converter-dominated systems. While GFL units are limited in their ability to support system dynamics, GFM control parameters—such as virtual inertia, droop, and damping—directly influence key stability indicators, including RoCoF, frequency nadir, and voltage recovery. This forms the basis for the analyses performed in this thesis.

\section{GFM Control Methods}

Grid-forming (GFM) inverters regulate voltage and frequency by emulating the behaviour of voltage sources. Their control structure is designed to ensure stable operation and grid code compliance under disturbances while maintaining power balance and voltage support. \cite{10755449, 10620991}. Their control framework is generally organised around three main objectives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Power control:} Regulation of active and reactive power to maintain the balance between generation and demand.
    \item \textbf{Frequency regulation:} Adjustment of inverter output during frequency deviations to support frequency restoration.
    \item \textbf{Voltage control:} Reactive power modulation to support voltage stability during transients.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Droop Control}

Droop control establishes a proportional relationship between frequency and active power, and between voltage and reactive power \cite{10620991}. By using \(P\text{--}f\) and \(Q\text{--}V\) characteristics, multiple inverters can share load without communication, which makes the method robust and suitable for decentralised operation.

Although droop control can regulate voltage and frequency effectively, it does not reproduce the synchronous machine swing dynamics. As a consequence, conventional droop controllers do not inherently provide inertia support.

The droop dynamics used in GFM control are commonly expressed as \cite{10949993}:
\begin{equation}
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\omega &= \omega_{\text{ref}} + K_P (P_{\text{ref}} - P), \\
V &= V_{\text{ref}} + K_Q (Q_{\text{ref}} - Q).
\end{aligned}
\right.
\end{equation}

Here, \(K_P\) and \(K_Q\) denote the active- and reactive-power droop coefficients, while \(P\), \(Q\), \(V\), and \(\omega\) represent the measured quantities. This strategy enables decentralised and reliable load sharing, although its dynamic performance is limited by the absence of inertia emulation.

\subsection{Virtual Synchronous Machine}

The Virtual Synchronous Machine (VSM), also referred to as Virtual Synchronous Generator (VSG), aims to emulate the electromechanical behaviour of a synchronous machine. This includes reproducing the rotor dynamics described by the swing equation, which enables the inverter to provide virtual inertia, damping, and improved transient voltage and frequency stability.

A representative formulation of VSM behaviour is \cite{10949993}:
\begin{align}
\dot{\theta}^{ref} &= \omega, \\
\dot{\omega} &= \frac{1}{J \omega} (P^{ref} - P) + \frac{D_p}{J} (\omega^{*} - \omega),
\end{align}

where \(J\) and \(D_p\) denote the virtual inertia and damping, respectively. By replicating synchronous machine dynamics, VSM-based control avoids rapid variations in frequency and voltage during disturbances. However, tuning the inertia and damping parameters is more complex than in droop control, and improper tuning may affect stability \cite{10755449}.

\section{Power System Stability}

Electric power systems are undergoing significant changes due to the increasing penetration of inverter-based resources (IBRs). These technologies introduce fast dynamics, reduced inertia, and new interactions between converter control systems and the grid, while the underlying mathematical framework of stability remains consistent with classical dynamic system theory \cite{1318675, 9286772}.

Historically, system dynamics were dominated by synchronous machines, whose inertia and electromechanical behaviour ensured predictable responses to disturbances. In contrast, modern systems with high shares of IBRs exhibit increased sensitivity to disturbances, particularly under weak-grid conditions characterised by low short-circuit ratio (SCR), where control interactions become more pronounced \cite{9286772}.

Power system stability is defined as the ability of the electrical network to return to an acceptable operating equilibrium after a disturbance, with system variables remaining within permissible limits and the network remaining intact \cite{1318675}. In converter-dominated systems, this capability is strongly influenced by converter control strategies and grid strength.

The increasing share of IBRs introduces additional challenges, including reduced short-circuit current, limited inherent inertia, and stronger interactions between converter control loops and network impedance. These effects can significantly impact both frequency and voltage stability, particularly in weak-grid conditions \cite{9286772}.


Different types of disturbances activate system components with distinct response times. Figure~\ref{fig:Power System Time Scales} illustrates the typical time scales of relevant dynamic phenomena.

IBRs exhibit significantly faster dynamic responses than synchronous machines. This motivates the inclusion of faster time scales when assessing systems with high IBR penetration \cite{9286772}.

\subsection{Categories of Stability}

Power system stability can be broadly classified into frequency and voltage stability, which are the primary focus of this work.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Frequency Stability:} Ability of the system to maintain or restore nominal frequency following power imbalances between generation and demand.
    \item \textbf{Voltage Stability:} Ability of the system to maintain acceptable voltage levels following disturbances, depending on reactive power availability and network conditions.
\end{itemize}

Other stability categories, such as rotor angle stability and resonance phenomena, are not explicitly addressed in this work, as the focus is placed on converter-dominated system behaviour.

\subsubsection{Converter-Driven Stability}

The increasing penetration of converter-interfaced renewable generation introduces new dynamic interactions driven primarily by control systems rather than physical inertia. In such systems, stability becomes highly dependent on controller design and grid strength.

In weak-grid conditions, interactions between converter control loops and network impedance may lead to poorly damped oscillations or even instability \cite{en14144290}. These phenomena span a wide frequency range and are particularly relevant in systems with high penetration of grid-forming and grid-following converters.

Fast converter-driven interactions often require electromagnetic transient (EMT) modelling for accurate representation, as they involve dynamics that are not captured by RMS models. However, EMT simulations require small time steps and are computationally intensive.

Overall, the increasing share of IBRs requires modelling approaches capable of capturing both system-level dynamics and converter-driven interactions. While EMT simulations provide detailed insights into fast dynamics, their computational cost limits their application to smaller systems. Therefore, efficient RMS-based approaches, complemented by EMT validation, are essential for analysing the impact of GFM control parameters under varying grid strength conditions.

\section{RMS and EMT Approaches}

Transient stability analysis evaluates the nonlinear dynamic response of a power system subjected to large disturbances, such as short circuits or the sudden loss of generation. In classical studies, the faulted element is cleared by protection systems, and the post-fault dynamics determine whether the system remains stable \cite{kundur1994}.

Phasor Domain Transient (PDT) or RMS simulations represent voltages and currents as complex phasors—magnitudes and angles—rather than instantaneous waveforms. Because three-phase steady-state quantities can be related to their phasor representation, RMS simulations avoid solving electromagnetic phenomena explicitly, resulting in high computational efficiency. For decades, this approach has been the standard for transient stability studies due to its speed and suitability for large-scale system analysis and operator decision support.

However, RMS models rely on quasi–steady-state assumptions. They capture only fundamental-frequency electromechanical behaviour and neglect fast electromagnetic dynamics (“network transients”), limiting their ability to represent interactions driven by power electronic converters \cite{badrzadeh2020power}.

Electromagnetic transient (EMT) programs provide a more detailed representation by solving instantaneous differential equations, typically using Dommel’s algorithm. EMT simulations capture both electromechanical and electromagnetic energy exchanges in network components, including the dynamics of inductances, capacitances, controllers, and switching elements \cite{arrillaga2003}. This makes EMT indispensable for analysing fast converter-driven interactions, resonance phenomena, and weak-grid behaviour—topics increasingly relevant in systems with high IBR penetration.

Electromagnetic transients arise from interactions between electric and magnetic fields and evolve on sub-millisecond time scales. Electromechanical transients, in contrast, originate from the dynamics of synchronous machine rotors and operate over hundreds of milliseconds or seconds. An intermediate region exists where both phenomena interact and may require combined modelling approaches \cite{arrillaga2003}. In practice, transient waveforms consist of oscillatory components associated with different natural frequencies, and selecting the appropriate modelling domain depends on the time-scale of interest.

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency range of coverage EMT and PDT studies} summarises the frequency ranges covered by RMS and EMT approaches. RMS studies are suitable for electromechanical stability assessment, while EMT simulations are required to capture fast converter and controller dynamics—particularly relevant for weak grids and grid-forming inverter analysis.

\section{Frequency and Voltage Stability Indicators}

Power system stability analysis requires quantitative indicators to evaluate the system response to disturbances and its ability to return to an acceptable operating point. In converter-dominated systems, reduced inertia and faster dynamics increase the importance of such indicators for assessing control strategies and grid strength.

\subsection{Frequency Stability Indicators}

Frequency stability refers to the ability of the system to maintain nominal frequency following disturbances such as generation outages or load changes. In traditional systems, inertia limits the initial frequency deviation, while primary control determines the steady-state behaviour \cite{kundur1994}. In systems with high shares of inverter-based resources, reduced inertia makes frequency indicators particularly critical.

\subsubsection{Theoretical Background}

System frequency is determined by the balance between mechanical input and electrical output power. A disturbance leads to a frequency deviation governed by the swing equation:
\begin{equation}
    2H \frac{df}{dt} = P_m - P_e,
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the inertia constant.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/frequency response of generating unit.png}
\caption{Frequency response of a generating unit \cite{kundur1994}.}
\label{fig:Frequency Respone of a generating unit.}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency Respone of a generating unit.} illustrates the inertial response, frequency nadir, and recovery following a disturbance.

\subsubsection{Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF)}

RoCoF quantifies the initial rate of frequency change and is a key indicator of system inertia and robustness. It can be approximated as \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{RoCoF}\big|_{t=0^+} = 
    \frac{\Delta P_{\mathrm{Imbalance}}}{P_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \cdot \frac{f_0}{2H}.
\end{equation}

High RoCoF values may lead to protection maloperation or generator tripping, and are therefore constrained by grid codes \cite{ENTSOE2020_RoCoF}.

\subsubsection{Frequency Nadir}

The frequency nadir is the minimum frequency reached after a disturbance and reflects the combined effect of inertia and primary control \cite{kundur1994}. It is a critical indicator for avoiding under-frequency load shedding.

\subsubsection{Steady-State Frequency Deviation}

Following primary control, the system settles at a new equilibrium frequency:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{\mathrm{ss}} = 
    -\frac{\Delta P_L}{\frac{1}{R} + D}.
\end{equation}

This indicator reflects the adequacy of primary frequency control reserves.

Overall, RoCoF and nadir are the primary indicators used in this work to assess frequency stability under different control strategies and grid strength conditions.

\subsection{Voltage Stability Indicators}

Voltage stability refers to the ability of the system to maintain acceptable voltages under normal conditions and after disturbances. It is primarily determined by the balance of reactive power and network strength \cite{kundur1994}.

The fundamental mechanisms of voltage stability can be illustrated using the classical $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ characteristics, which describe the relationship between power transfer, reactive power, and voltage.

\begin{figure}[htbp!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{Figures/Chapter 2/PV and QV curves.png}
\caption{PV and QV curves.}
\label{fig:PV QV Curves}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:PV QV Curves} shows the $P$--$V$ and $Q$--$V$ curves, where the nose point defines the stability limit and the slope indicates voltage sensitivity.

\subsubsection{Voltage Magnitude Deviation}

Voltage deviation quantifies the difference between the instantaneous and pre-disturbance voltage:
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_i(t) = V_i(t) - V_{i,0}.
\end{equation}

The maximum deviation is
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_{i,\max} = \max_t \left| \Delta V_i(t) \right|.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Post-Fault Voltage Dip}

The voltage dip is the minimum voltage reached during and after a disturbance:
\begin{equation}
V_{\min} = \min_{t \in [t_{\mathrm{fault}},\, t_{\mathrm{end}}]} V(t).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Voltage Recovery Time}

Voltage recovery time measures how fast the system returns to acceptable voltage levels:
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{rec}} = 
\min \left\{ t > t_{\mathrm{clear}} \;\big|\; \left| V(t) - V_0 \right| \leq \epsilon \right\}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Voltage Oscillation Damping Ratio}

The damping ratio characterises how fast post-disturbance oscillations decay:
\begin{equation}
\delta = \ln\left( \frac{x_1}{x_2} \right), \quad
\zeta = \frac{\delta}{\sqrt{4\pi^2 + \delta^2}}.
\end{equation}

This indicator is used to assess the effect of control parameters on oscillatory behaviour in weak grids.

Overall, voltage dip, recovery time, and damping are the key indicators used in this work to evaluate voltage stability under different GFM parameter settings and short-circuit ratios.

\section{Grid Strength and Short-Circuit Ratio}


Grid strength is a fundamental concept in power system stability analysis and describes the ability of a network to maintain acceptable voltage and frequency conditions under disturbances. According to IEEE, grid strength can be quantified using the short-circuit power or the short-circuit ratio (SCR), defined as the ratio between the short-circuit power at the point of interconnection and the rated power of the connected inverter-based resource \cite{ieee2800}:
\begin{equation}
    SCR = \frac{S_{SC}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}.
\end{equation}

CIGRÉ refines this concept by classifying grids as strong ($SCR > 10$), weak ($SCR < 5$), and very weak ($SCR < 3$), and introduces the effective SCR (ESCR) to account for reactive compensation devices \cite{cigreTB871}. Classical references such as Kundur and Anderson \& Fouad do not define SCR explicitly for converters, but they provide the theoretical basis for electrical stiffness, voltage sensitivity, and short-circuit strength as key determinants of system stability \cite{kundur1994,anderson2003}.

System operators increasingly use SCR as a practical stability metric. AEMO considers $SCR < 3$ critical for grid-following inverters, ERCOT specifies minimum ESCR levels for HVDC and wind integration, and ENTSO-E associates low SCR conditions with an increased risk of oscillations, FRT failures, PLL instability, and poor voltage regulation \cite{aemo2018,ercotHVDC,entsoe2016}. In converter-dominated systems, SCR is therefore particularly relevant, since weak grids amplify the sensitivity of dynamic behaviour to control parameters such as virtual inertia, damping, and droop gains, directly influencing the performance of grid-forming inverters in both RMS and EMT simulations.

The main quantities used to characterise grid strength are:
\begin{equation}
S_{SC} = \sqrt{3} \, V_{\mathrm{nom}} \, I_{SC},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
Z_{\mathrm{th}} = \frac{V_{\mathrm{nom}}^{2}}{S_{SC}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
ESCR = \frac{S_{SC} - Q_{\mathrm{comp}}}{P_{\mathrm{rated}}}.
\end{equation}

Here, $S_{SC}$ is the short-circuit power at the point of interconnection, obtained from the nominal voltage $V_{\mathrm{nom}}$ and the short-circuit current $I_{SC}$ of a fault study. The equivalent Thevenin impedance $Z_{\mathrm{th}}$ represents the electrical stiffness of the grid as “seen” from the connection point. In systems with shunt reactive compensation $Q_{\mathrm{comp}}$, the effective SCR (ESCR) provides a corrected measure by subtracting the contribution of capacitive reactive power. These relationships form the quantitative basis used in this thesis to classify grids as strong, weak, or very weak and to analyse the impact of GFM control parameters across different SCR levels.

\section{Small signal stability}

Small-signal stability refers to the ability of a power system to maintain synchronism when subjected to small disturbances. A disturbance is considered small if the equations describing the resulting system response can be linearized around an operating point for the purpose of analysis \cite{Kundur}.

Small-signal instability may occur in two forms: (i) a steady increase in generator rotor angle due to insufficient synchronizing torque, and (ii) rotor oscillations of increasing amplitude due to insufficient damping torque.

The dynamic behaviour of a power system can be described by a set of $n$ first-order nonlinear differential equations of the form

\begin{equation}
\dot{x}_i = f_i(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n;\,u_1,u_2,\ldots,u_r;\,t), 
\qquad i = 1,2,\ldots,n
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the order of the system and $r$ is the number of inputs. In vector-matrix notation, this can be written as

\begin{equation}
\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{u},t)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Linearization}

Small-signal stability analysis is performed by linearizing the nonlinear system equations around an equilibrium operating point $(\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{u}_0)$ that satisfies

\begin{equation}
\dot{\mathbf{x}}_0 = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{u}_0) = 0
\end{equation}

Small perturbations around this equilibrium are defined as

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_0 + \Delta \mathbf{x}, \qquad
\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}_0 + \Delta \mathbf{u}
\end{equation}

Assuming the perturbations are small, the nonlinear function $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{u})$ can be approximated using a first-order Taylor series expansion while neglecting higher-order terms. This results in the linearized state-space representation

\begin{equation}
\Delta \dot{\mathbf{x}} = A \Delta \mathbf{x} + B \Delta \mathbf{u}
\end{equation}

where the matrices $A$ and $B$ are the Jacobians of the system equations

\begin{equation}
A = \left. \frac{\partial \mathbf{f}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \right|_{(\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{u}_0)},
\qquad
B = \left. \frac{\partial \mathbf{f}}{\partial \mathbf{u}} \right|_{(\mathbf{x}_0,\mathbf{u}_0)}.
\end{equation}

The small-signal stability of the system is determined by the eigenvalues of the state matrix $A$. These eigenvalues are defined as the values of the scalar parameter $\lambda$ for which non-trivial solutions exist for

\begin{equation}
A\boldsymbol{\phi} = \lambda \boldsymbol{\phi}
\end{equation}

This equation can be rewritten as

\begin{equation}
(A-\lambda I)\boldsymbol{\phi} = 0
\end{equation}

For a non-trivial solution to exist, the determinant must satisfy the characteristic equation

\begin{equation}
\det(A-\lambda I) = 0
\end{equation}

The $n$ solutions $\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n$ are the eigenvalues of $A$.

A real eigenvalue $\lambda = \sigma$ corresponds to a non-oscillatory mode. Negative real eigenvalues represent an exponentially decaying response; the larger the magnitude, the faster the decay. Positive values indicate aperiodic instability.

Complex conjugate eigenvalues $\lambda = \sigma \pm j\omega$ correspond to oscillatory modes, where $\sigma$ determines the damping and $\omega$ determines the oscillation frequency. The frequency of oscillation is given by

\begin{equation}
f = \frac{\omega}{2 \pi}
\end{equation}

which represents the damped frequency. The damping ratio is given by

\begin{equation}
\zeta = \frac{-\sigma}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \omega^2}}
\end{equation}

which determines the rate of decay of the oscillation amplitude.

The eigenvalues determine the time behaviour, i.e., the frequency and rate of decay of the natural modes of the system \cite{Elwood1991Eigenanalysis}.

Each eigenvalue corresponds to a dynamic mode of the system, representing a specific oscillatory or non-oscillatory behaviour involving a combination of system state variables such as generator rotor angles, speeds, or controller states. In conventional power systems, the dominant small-signal modes are typically electromechanical oscillations associated with generator rotor angles and speeds. Therefore, the location of eigenvalues in the complex plane provides information about the frequency and damping of the system modes and is widely used to assess small-signal stability. 
Modal analysis techniques such as participation factors, eigenvector analysis, and measures of controllability and observability can be used to identify the contribution of individual state variables to each mode. However, this work focuses on the dominant eigenvalues and their associated damping characteristics as indicators of system stability.

\subsection{Research Gap and Motivation}
The objective of this thesis is to evaluate frequency and voltage stability in a converter-dominated power system and to analyse how key GFM control parameters—virtual inertia, damping, and droop—behave under different grid strength scenarios. By assessing parameter sensitivity across strong, weak, and very weak grids, this work aims to provide a clear understanding of how GFM tuning affects system-level stability indicators. A brief overview of market-related considerations is also included to contextualize the practical relevance of GFM operation. Ultimately, the goal is to provide RWE with a consistent and transparent assessment framework to quantify the impact of GFM parameters on system stability and to offer insights into how these findings may influence future grid market designs and RWE's potential participation in them.


\section{Transfer Capability Analysis}

Transmission networks enable the exchange of electrical power between geographically separated regions. However, the amount of power that can be transferred through the transmission network is limited by physical constraints such as thermal limits of transmission lines, voltage limits, and system stability requirements. In addition, system operators must ensure that these limits are respected not only in the base operating condition but also under credible contingencies. Therefore, determining the maximum transferable power between two areas is an important task in both transmission system planning and operation \cite{wood2013power,kundur1994power}.

The concept of transfer capability is commonly used to quantify the maximum amount of power that can be exchanged between two regions of a power system while maintaining secure system operation. In practical applications, the Total Transfer Capability (TTC) represents the maximum power transfer that can be accommodated without violating system operating limits. Considering operational uncertainties, a transmission reliability margin (TRM) is typically introduced, resulting in the Available Transfer Capability (ATC), which represents the remaining transmission capacity available for additional transactions \cite{taylor1994power,nerc1996atc}.
Security assessment of transfer capability is typically based on the verification of operating limits in both the base-case condition (\(N\)) and under single-component outage conditions (\(N-1\)). The \(N\) condition represents normal system operation with all relevant network elements in service, whereas the \(N-1\) criterion requires that the system remains within acceptable operating limits after the outage of any one critical component, such as a transmission line, transformer, or generator \cite{kundur1994power,wood2013power}. In practice, the maximum transferable power is therefore not only limited by the pre-contingency operating point, but also by post-contingency violations such as line overloads, unacceptable voltage deviations, or loss of stability. For this reason, contingency analysis is an essential part of transfer capability assessment.

In power system analysis tools such as DIgSILENT PowerFactory, transfer capability analysis determines the maximum power transfer between two regions or between sets of generators and loads by progressively scaling generation or demand until a network limit is reached \cite{digsilent2024pfmanual}. The limiting factors may include line thermal limits, voltage constraints, or violations that occur under contingency conditions.

\subsection{Power Transfer Distribution Factors}

To understand how a transfer between two regions affects the network, sensitivity factors known as Power Transfer Distribution Factors (PTDFs) are commonly used. PTDFs describe how a change in power injection between two regions affects the flows in transmission lines, providing a linear approximation of power flow redistribution \cite{wood2013power,stott2009dc}.

Mathematically, a PTDF represents the change in active power flow on a transmission line resulting from a unit change in power transfer between two nodes or regions. These factors allow the identification of network elements that are most affected by a transaction and therefore may become limiting elements in transfer capability studies.

In the context of transfer capability analysis, PTDFs are used to evaluate the change in power flows across a set of branches when production in one region and consumption in another region are modified. Knowledge of these sensitivity factors assists in identifying the network elements that constrain cross-border transmission capability \cite{digsilent2024pfmanual}.

Additionally, flow decomposition techniques can be applied to determine the contribution of individual regions or transactions to the loading of transmission elements, enabling the identification of which transfers are responsible for network congestion.

\subsection{Transfer Capability Analysis in PowerFactory}

DIgSILENT PowerFactory provides a dedicated Transfer Capability Analysis tool to evaluate the maximum transferable power between two areas of a power system. The method determines the transfer limit by incrementally scaling generation and/or load until a network constraint becomes active \cite{digsilent2024pfmanual}.

Two main approaches are available for defining the transfer interface. In the \textit{region-based} option, importing and exporting regions are defined by grouping network elements into predefined areas. The transfer capability is then calculated for the transmission lines connecting the two regions or across a user-defined boundary. Alternatively, the \textit{element-based} option allows the user to explicitly define a boundary consisting of selected network elements for which the transfer capability is evaluated.

During the analysis, generation in the exporting region is increased while demand in the importing region is increased correspondingly. This incremental transfer continues until operational limits such as thermal ratings, voltage limits, or contingency violations are reached. The corresponding transfer level represents the maximum allowable power exchange between the defined regions.
