\subsubsection{Current Contribution During the Fault}

The short-circuit current contribution differs significantly among the analyzed technologies. The synchronous generator (SG) exhibits the highest current injection, reaching values of approximately $5\,\text{pu}$. This behavior is consistent with the physical characteristics of synchronous machines, where the internal voltage source and low subtransient impedance lead to large fault current contributions.

The grid-forming converter (GFM) shows an intermediate response. An initial current peak of approximately $1.8$--$1.9\,\text{pu}$ is observed, followed by a rapid limitation to around $1.2\,\text{pu}$ for the duration of the fault. This behavior is associated with the implemented current limiting mechanisms and virtual impedance control, which restrict the converter current to protect the hardware, as can be seen in \ref{fig:PositiveSeqCurrent}

In contrast, the grid-following converter (GFL) maintains a nearly constant current close to its nominal value (approximately $1\,\text{pu}$) throughout the fault. 

These results highlight the fundamental differences between synchronous and converter-based technologies in terms of short-circuit current contribution.

On the other hand, the observed current behavior reflects the inherent limitations of converter-based technologies. Both GFM and GFL units are subject to current constraints, although implemented differently. In the GFM case, the limitation is actively enforced through control mechanisms such as virtual impedance and voltage control loops. In the GFL case, the limitation is intrinsic to its current-controlled structure.

As a result, converter-based units do not provide fault current support comparable to synchronous machines, which has important implications for protection systems and grid strength.

\subsubsection{Post-Fault Dynamic Response}

After the fault clearance at $t=1.1$ s, distinct dynamic behaviors are observed. The synchronous generator exhibits pronounced oscillations in the current response, reflecting its electromechanical nature and the interaction between rotor dynamics and the network. \ref{fig:PositiveSeqCurrent}

The grid-forming converter demonstrates a smoother and more damped recovery. The current gradually returns to steady-state values without significant oscillations, indicating effective damping introduced by the control strategy.

The grid-following converter shows a delayed recovery characterized by a temporary drop in current after fault clearing, followed by a gradual return to steady-state conditions. This behavior is associated with the re-synchronization process of the phase-locked loop (PLL).



\subsubsection{Voltage Response}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{Figures/Chapter 7/Phase1/threephsc/VatPCR.png}
    \caption{Voltage response at PCR during a three phase short circuit}
    \label{fig:Vpcr}
\end{figure}

The voltage at the PCR drops to approximately $0\,\text{pu}$ during the fault for all technologies, which is consistent with the modeled solid fault. After fault clearance, differences in voltage recovery are observed. The highest overshoots are observed in the grid-forming VSM and GFL IEC models, reaching values of up to $1.126\,\text{pu}$ and $1.107\,\text{pu}$, respectively. The synchronous generator exhibits a more moderate overshoot of approximately $1.027\,\text{pu}$, while the grid-forming droop-controlled converter shows no significant overshoot, remaining close to $1.0\,\text{pu}$.

In terms of voltage settling time, the fastest responses are observed for the converter-based technologies with fast control dynamics, particularly the GFM VSM and SMA-based models, which settle within approximately $0.04$--$0.06\,\text{s}$. The grid-forming droop control also shows a relatively fast response with a settling time of $0.148\,\text{s}$. In contrast, the synchronous generator and GFL IEC model exhibit slower voltage recovery, with settling times of $0.426\,\text{s}$ and $0.437\,\text{s}$, respectively, while the GFL WECC model shows the slowest response, reaching $0.534\,\text{s}$.

These results indicate that converter-based technologies are capable of achieving significantly faster voltage recovery due to their control-based response, whereas synchronous machines exhibit slower dynamics associated with their electromechanical nature. Additionally, the magnitude of the voltage overshoot is influenced by the control strategy, where faster control action may lead to larger transient excursions if not sufficiently damped. \ref{fig:Vpcr}

\subsubsection{Conclusion}

The comparison demonstrates that synchronous generators, grid-forming converters, and grid-following converters exhibit fundamentally different behaviors under fault conditions. Grid-forming converters provide a compromise between fault current contribution and stable dynamic performance, while grid-following converters offer limited support to the grid during disturbances.

\subsubsection{Initial Frequency Dynamics}

The initial frequency dynamics following the disturbance are characterized by the rate of change of frequency (RoCoF). The results show that VSM-based control achieves the lowest RoCoF, closely resembling the behaviour of synchronous machines due to inertia emulation. The synchronous generator exhibits a similarly low RoCoF, confirming its inherent inertial response.

In contrast, grid-following converters exhibit higher RoCoF values, as they do not inherently provide inertia and instead follow the grid conditions through synchronization mechanisms. This leads to faster frequency deviations immediately after the disturbance.

These results highlight the importance of inertia, whether physical or emulated, in limiting the initial frequency deviation.

\subsubsection{Frequency Nadir and Active Power Response}

The frequency nadir is influenced not only by inertia but also by the speed and magnitude of the active power response. Although the VSM exhibits the lowest RoCoF, it does not achieve the best frequency nadir. This is due to its relatively slower active power response compared to droop-controlled systems.

The GFM droop control achieves a slightly better nadir, as it provides a faster active power injection following the disturbance. In contrast, the GFL-WECC model exhibits the worst nadir, which can be attributed to its limited capability to provide primary frequency support.

These observations demonstrate that a lower RoCoF does not necessarily imply improved frequency stability, as the nadir depends on both inertial response and primary frequency control.

\subsubsection{Frequency Recovery and Settling Time}

The settling time reflects the ability of the system to restore frequency to steady-state conditions. The results show that synchronous generators and VSM-based converters exhibit longer settling times, which are associated with their inertial and electromechanical dynamics.

Grid-following converters, on the other hand, tend to settle faster, as they do not actively participate in frequency regulation and therefore do not introduce additional dynamic interactions.

The GFL-WECC model exhibits the longest settling time, which is associated with an overshoot in the frequency response that delays convergence. This behaviour indicates a less damped response compared to the other technologies.

\subsubsection{Steady-State Frequency}

At steady state, all grid-following units converge to the same final frequency value. This reflects their passive role in frequency control, as they do not contribute to balancing the power mismatch in steady-state conditions.

In contrast, small deviations in steady-state frequency are observed for grid-forming converters and the synchronous generator. This is a consequence of their active participation in frequency regulation through droop characteristics, which determine the final operating point of the system.

\subsubsection{Discussion}

The results indicate that frequency stability is governed by a combination of inertial response and active power control. While inertia (physical or emulated) is essential to limit the initial frequency deviation, the frequency nadir and overall system stability depend strongly on the effectiveness of primary frequency control.

Grid-forming converters provide a balanced response by combining inertia emulation with active power support, whereas grid-following converters offer limited contribution to frequency stability. The observed behaviour also highlights the importance of control tuning, particularly in converter-based technologies, where different implementations can lead to significantly different dynamic responses.

The results show that the dominant damped frequency remains close to 1~Hz for the majority of the analyzed operating scenarios. This frequency range is characteristic of electromechanical oscillations typically observed in large interconnected power systems and is mainly associated with the rotor dynamics of synchronous machines.

Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvalue_evolution} illustrates the evolution of the dominant eigenvalues in the complex plane. In these cases, participation factor analysis was performed in order to identify the states contributing most strongly to the unstable mode and to better understand the underlying mechanism driving the instability.

\subsection{Emergence of an Unstable Mode}

At scenario R07, the system becomes unstable even in the absence of disturbances. The eigenvalue analysis reveals the appearance of a positive real eigenvalue ($\sigma > 0$), indicating loss of small-signal stability.

Unlike the previously observed oscillatory modes, the unstable mode is non-oscillatory and corresponds to a purely real eigenvalue. Participation factor analysis indicates that this mode is dominated by internal states associated with the phase-locked loop (PLL) of the grid-following converters.

This behavior suggests that at very high penetration levels, grid-following converters struggle to maintain synchronization due to the reduced presence of synchronous machines providing a strong voltage and frequency reference.

\subsection{Stabilization Through Grid-Forming Converters}

Additional scenarios were evaluated by introducing grid-forming converters into the system. The results for scenarios R06A, R06B, R06BA, R07A, R07B, R08A demonstrate that the introduction of grid-forming units shifts the dominant eigenvalues further into the left half-plane.

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:dominant_modes}, the damping ratios increase significantly in these scenarios, reaching values between approximately 7\% and 12\%.

This improvement in damping indicates that grid-forming converters provide additional stabilizing effects through their voltage and frequency control mechanisms, partially replacing the stabilizing role traditionally provided by synchronous machines.

Furthermore, in the final scenarios (R09 and R09A), where the synchronous slack generator is replaced by grid-forming converters, the dominant oscillation frequency decreases to approximately 0.6~Hz. This represents a noticeable reduction compared to the $\sim$1~Hz electromechanical mode observed in the preceding scenarios. The shift toward a lower oscillation frequency indicates a change in the dominant system dynamics as the share of converter-based generation increases.

\subsection{Summary of Observed Stability Limits}

Based on the performed analysis, three key observations regarding system stability can be identified.

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Dynamic robustness limit:} Between scenarios R05 and R06, where the system begins to lose the ability to withstand large disturbances despite remaining locally stable according to small-signal analysis.

\item \textbf{Small-signal stability limit:} Between scenarios R06 and R07, where a positive real eigenvalue appears and the operating point becomes unstable.

\item \textbf{Stabilization through grid-forming converters:} In scenarios where grid-forming converters are introduced (e.g., R06A, R07A, and R08A), the dominant eigenvalues move back to the left half-plane and the damping of the dominant oscillatory mode improves, restoring system stability.

\end{itemize}

Overall, the results highlight the increasing influence of converter control dynamics in power systems with high shares of converter-based generation. High penetrations of grid-following converters can lead to reduced stability margins and can ultimately result in instability. The participation factor analysis of the unstable scenarios indicates that the dominant states were associated with the phase-locked loop (PLL), which confirms that grid-following converters rely on a sufficiently stiff grid for stable operation.

Furthermore, increasing penetration of converter-based generation leads to a progressive shift of the eigenvalues in the complex plane. From a system-theoretic perspective, this reflects a modification of the system state matrix, implying that the overall dynamic representation of the power system changes as synchronous machines are replaced by converter-interfaced resources. As a result, the characteristics of the dominant oscillatory modes may also change.

Finally the results also show that the integration of grid-forming converters can significantly improve system stability. In scenarios such as R06B and R06BA, the introduction of grid-forming capability increases modal damping and restores dynamic robustness. However, the results further indicate that the location of grid-forming units within the network influences the system dynamics, suggesting that the allocation of these assets plays an important role in the stability of converter-dominated power systems.

These results highlight the limitations of GFL converters in systems with high penetration of inverter-based resources. \subsection{Stabilizing Effect of Grid Forming Converters}

From this point onward, the analysis investigates whether grid-forming (GFM) converters are capable of restoring system stability.

In case R06A, one GFM unit based on a virtual synchronous machine (VSM) control was introduced. Instead of adding a GFL unit, the corresponding converter was replaced by a GFM unit while maintaining the same rated apparent power, dispatched active power, and voltage control strategy. In this way, the GFM unit delivers the same power as the replaced GFL unit.

The introduction of this GFM unit produced only a small improvement in the voltage dip; however, the recovery time of the voltage signal improved significantly. The recovery time decreased from 1.713 s in case R06 (with only GFL units) to 0.698 s in case R06A. This indicates a substantial improvement in the damping of post-fault oscillations. The RMS solver in PowerFactory also did not report convergence problems, suggesting that the system regained dynamic robustness with the addition of the GFM unit.

A similar effect is observed in case R07A. In the original R07 scenario the system could not reach a stable operating point. After replacing the GFL unit with a GFM unit, the system response becomes stable and the RMS simulation converges successfully. This demonstrates that GFM converters can restore dynamic robustness in converter-dominated systems.

In case R08, corresponding to a PV penetration of 75.23\%, the system again becomes unstable when only one GFM unit is present. Small-signal analysis reveals an unstable eigenvalue and the RMS simulation fails to converge. However, when a second VSM-based GFM unit is introduced in case R08A, the system regains dynamic robustness and stable operation is recovered.

These results indicate that GFM converters can extend the feasible penetration range of inverter-based resources in the system.

\subsection{Influence of GFM Placement}

Further simulations were performed to investigate the influence of the spatial placement of GFM units within the network.

When two GFM units were placed at buses close to each other (case R06B), the simulation produced convergence warnings after the fault was cleared. Although the voltage dip during the fault improved compared to the single-GFM case, the post-fault dynamics showed numerical instability. This behavior suggests that unfavorable interactions between the control loops of nearby GFM units may occur.

To evaluate this effect, a second configuration was tested in case R06BA, where the second GFM unit was relocated to Bus 36. In this scenario, the simulation converged successfully and the voltage recovery improved. The recovery time was reduced by approximately 17\% compared to the single-GFM case, indicating improved damping of post-fault oscillations.

\subsection{Limits of Stability Restoration}

Finally, case R09 represents an extreme scenario in which the slack generator is replaced, resulting in approximately 83\% inverter-based generation in the system.

Although small-signal analysis shows that all eigenvalues remain in the left half of the complex plane, the system is still unable to withstand the three-phase fault. During the disturbance, pole slip of the remaining synchronous generator is observed. This indicates that restoring small-signal stability does not necessarily guarantee transient robustness under severe disturbances.

Increasing the share of GFM units therefore extends the feasible penetration range of inverter-based resources. However, beyond a certain point, restoring operating-point feasibility and small-signal stability is no longer sufficient to guarantee transient fault stability. In other words, at very high converter penetration levels, simply adding more GFM units does not necessarily ensure adequate fault ride-through capability.
