\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:Introduction}



The ongoing energy transition is driving a fundamental transformation of power systems towards cleaner and more sustainable electricity generation. This transition is characterized by a rapid increase in renewable energy sources, such as wind and photovoltaic systems, which are predominantly connected to the grid through power electronic converters, commonly referred to as inverter-based resources (IBRs). 

Unlike conventional synchronous machines, IBRs do not inherently provide rotational inertia or natural damping, as they are decoupled from the grid through converters. As synchronous generation is progressively displaced, power systems experience a reduction in inertia and short-circuit strength, leading to increased vulnerability to frequency and voltage instability, particularly under large disturbances.

IBRs can be broadly classified into grid-following (GFL) and grid-forming (GFM) converters. GFL converters operate as controlled current sources synchronized to the grid via a phase-locked loop (PLL), which makes their performance highly dependent on grid strength. In contrast, GFM converters behave as controlled voltage sources capable of establishing voltage magnitude and frequency references independently. This fundamental difference enables GFM converters to contribute to system stability by providing functionalities such as frequency support, voltage regulation, and operation in weak or islanded conditions. As highlighted in \cite{digsilent2024pfmanual}, grid-forming control strategies, including droop control and virtual synchronous machine (VSM) concepts, emulate the behavior of synchronous machines by introducing virtual inertia and damping into converter dynamics.

Among the technologies implementing GFM control, battery energy storage systems (BESS) are of particular interest due to their fast dynamic response and bidirectional power capability. These characteristics make BESS well suited to support system stability during transient events while participating in ancillary service provision.

Despite the recognized potential of GFM converters, their system-level impact under different operating conditions remains an active area of research. In particular, the influence of control parameters such as virtual inertia, frequency droop, and damping on system stability is not yet fully understood, especially in weak grids characterized by low short-circuit ratios (SCR). Furthermore, differences between RMS and EMT simulation approaches can lead to discrepancies in the representation of fast transients and converter dynamics.

This thesis aims to investigate the impact of grid-forming converters on the dynamic stability of power systems with high penetration of inverter-based resources. The analysis is performed using RMS simulations in DIgSILENT PowerFactory. The study focuses on evaluating key stability indicators, including rate of change of frequency (RoCoF), frequency nadir, settling time, and voltage recovery, under disturbances such as load steps and short-circuit events.

In addition, the effect of GFM penetration levels on system dynamics is investigated using a modified IEEE 39-bus system, including small-signal stability and eigenvalue analysis.

Finally, transmission-level analyses based on Power Transfer Distribution Factors (PTDF) and Transfer Capability Analysis (TCA) are performed to evaluate how the integration and location of new generation sources impact power flows and network constraints. These analyses provide complementary insight into how local converter behavior translates into system-wide effects, particularly in terms of congestion and transfer limits.

Overall, this work provides a comprehensive assessment of grid-forming converter behavior in converter-dominated power systems and highlights the evolving nature of power systems driven by the energy transition.
